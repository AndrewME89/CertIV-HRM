# Syllabus

Human resources policies set a strategic direction for a particular area, and the policy outlines how the organisation must conduct business. The policy and procedure framework establishes a hierarchy, categorises the various policies, and the procedures describe how to undertake various requirements of the policies and list the required standards. Supporting documentation, such as audit checklists and reviews, provides the development steps to be taken and the improvement process.

The interpretation and documentation of key findings relating to policy and procedure frameworks cannot be undertaken until the various data is collated and analysed. Once the data is collated and analysed, the process of converting the various pieces of information and findings will need to be documented in a way that makes sense to the reader.

The data must be presented in a way that shows how the investigation has been undertaken, the data and information used, and the key findings after the analysis has been conducted.

There are a number of ways to document the key findings of the analysis and it depends on whether the analysis is based on quantitative data or qualitative investigation. Quantitative data is numeric and involves the use of numbers to describe the issue under investigation.

Qualitative data investigations are usually used to explore the things that appear behind the numbers, often focusing on the answers to the ‘why’ questions and dealing with quality. Quantitative data can be documented and presented through graphs, pie charts and other visual representations of data, including tables. For example:

|                       |                                      |                                                      |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Total number of staff | Number of staff over 55 years of age | Number of staff due to retire in the next five years |
| 843                   | 146                                  | 67                                                   |

The qualitative data and investigation can then help determine the ‘why’ behind the numeric data and provide further clarification and key findings of the data.
#### In the real world

“Roll On” is a large manufacturer of caravans. Due to an increase in sales, the human resources department has been asked by the CEO to conduct an analysis of employee workloads using both qualitative and quantitative data. The production team are given weekly and monthly targets, and the quantitative data can be used both to track employee performance in relation to these targets. It also provides an opportunity to review whether or not targets are realistic. An employee satisfaction survey is also conducted using qualitative data for analysing the more complex workloads and tasks whose completion cannot be easily quantified and where data and information need to be gathered in relation to the employee’s perceptions of the workloads, satisfaction levels and stress levels.
