**

## 1. What is Policy Review?

Definition:  
The process of regularly reviewing and updating organisational policies and procedures to ensure they remain:

- Accurate
    
- Relevant
    
- Compliant
    

---

### Key Insight:

Policies are living documents, not “set-and-forget”.

---

## 2. Why Policy Review Matters

---

### 1. Legal Compliance

- Ensures alignment with current laws and regulations
    

---

### 2. Risk Reduction

- Prevents legal issues, fines, and inconsistencies
    

---

### 3. Operational Relevance

- Keeps policies aligned with:
    

- Current systems
    
- Technology
    
- Work practices
    

---

### 4. Consistency & Clarity

- Helps employees understand expectations
    

---

### 5. Organisational Protection

- Protects:
    

- Employees
    
- Assets
    
- Reputation
    

---

### Key Insight:

Outdated policies = organisational risk

---

## 3. When to Review Policies

---

### 3.1 Scheduled Reviews

- Recommended:
    

- Every 1–3 years
    
- Best practice: annually
    

---

### 3.2 Event-Driven Reviews

Triggered by:

- Organisational changes (leadership, mergers)
    
- Legal or regulatory changes
    
- Incidents or policy violations
    
- Technology changes
    
- Process updates
    
- Audit findings
    

---

### Key Insight:

Best practice = scheduled + event-based reviews

---

## 4. Risks of Not Reviewing Policies

- Non-compliance with laws
    
- Outdated procedures
    
- Employee confusion
    
- Increased legal costs
    
- Poor adoption (employees ignore policies)
    

---

## 5. Identifying Policies That Need Updating

---

### Key Questions:

---

### 1. Is the Policy Being Followed?

If not:

- Is it outdated?
    
- Too complex?
    
- Poorly communicated?
    
- A training issue?
    

---

### 2. Is It Achieving Its Purpose?

- Are outcomes improving?
    
- Are goals being met?
    

---

### 3. Is It Current and Relevant?

- Matches current:
    

- Technology
    
- Work practices
    
- Laws
    

---

### Key Insight:

A policy can be followed perfectly and still fail.

---

## 6. Policy Review Process (Practical Steps)

---

### Step 1: Identify Policies to Review

- Prioritise:
    

- High-risk policies
    
- Legally required policies
    
- Critical operational policies
    

---

### Step 2: Assign Responsibility

- Define:
    

- Policy owner
    
- Review team
    

---

### Step 3: Gather Data & Feedback

- Input from:
    

- Managers
    
- HR
    
- Subject matter experts
    
- Frontline employees
    

---

### Step 4: Review & Revise Policy

- Update content
    
- Ensure compliance
    
- Align with operations
    

---

### Step 5: Approve Changes

- Management/legal sign-off
    

---

### Step 6: Communicate & Implement

- Distribute updated policies
    
- Train employees
    

---

### Step 7: Record & Track Changes

- Maintain documentation
    
- Version control
    
- Audit trail
    

---

## 7. Best Practices for Policy Review

---

### 1. Assign Policy Owners

- Clear accountability
    

---

### 2. Involve the Right People

- HR
    
- Managers
    
- SMEs
    
- Frontline staff
    

---

### 3. Use Documentation & Version Control

- Track:
    

- Changes
    
- Approvals
    
- Review history
    

---

### 4. Align with Current Operations

- Policies must reflect actual practice
    

---

### 5. Use Checklists

Example checklist:

- Policy owner identified
    
- Legal compliance verified
    
- Matches current operations
    
- Stakeholder feedback collected
    
- Changes documented
    
- Communicated to employees

## 10. Quick Summary (Memorise This)

Policy review is the ongoing process of updating organisational policies to ensure they remain compliant, relevant, and effective. Regular and event-driven reviews reduce risk, improve consistency, and support organisational performance.

## Straight-Up Reality Check

Most companies:

- Write policies once
    
- Never update them
    
- Wonder why no one follows them
    

Reality:

- If a policy doesn’t reflect real work → employees ignore it
    
- If it’s outdated → it’s a liability
    

Good HR keeps policies:

- Current
    
- Clear
    
- Actually usable
