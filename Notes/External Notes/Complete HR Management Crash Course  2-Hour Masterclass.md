## What is Human Resource Management ([[Human Resource Management (HRM)|HRM]])?

HRM is the process through which a company identifies, recruits, hires, positions, and manages its employees. It views the workforce as the organisation's most valuable resource — described as **human capital**.

**Primary purpose:** Help businesses meet immediate and long-term objectives by reducing risk and increasing [[Return on Investment (ROI)|ROI]].

HRM is not limited to one division — it's a cross-departmental role covering everyone from entry-level workers to executives.

---

## Scope of HRM — Key Functions

- **HR planning** — determining how many positions exist and whether there is a surplus or shortage of employees
- **Job analysis** — creating in-depth explanations of each role to inform job ads and recruitment
- **Recruitment and selection** — receiving applications, conducting interviews, choosing candidates
- **Orientation and induction** — introducing new employees to the company, its culture, values, and colleagues
- **Training and development** — improving work productivity for new and existing staff
- **Performance evaluation** — reviewing employee performance (typically annually) to inform pay, bonuses, and promotions
- **Compensation and benefits** — overseeing salaries, benefits packages, and perks
- **Employee motivation and wellbeing** — exploring techniques to keep staff engaged and healthy
- **Health and safety** — ensuring workplace safety rules are followed
- **Industrial relations** — maintaining positive relationships with unions to reduce risk of disputes or strike action

---

## Why HRM Matters

- Identifies staffing gaps and develops strategies to fill them
- Attracts, develops, and retains top talent
- Maximises employee output and productivity
- Maintains compliance with legal and regulatory requirements
- Builds a positive workplace culture that supports business goals

---

## Skills and Responsibilities of an HR Manager

- Oversees hiring practices, payroll, employee benefits, and workplace safety
- Sources, screens, and hires new personnel
- Administers employee benefit programs
- Requires strong interpersonal, communication, and collaborative skills
- Needs awareness of current trends in non-monetary perks and employee recognition
- Typically requires at least a bachelor's degree in HR or a related field (e.g. business)

## Global HRM — Three Staffing Approaches

|Approach|What it means|Pros|Cons|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Ethnocentric**|Fill key roles with parent company nationals|Unified culture; fills skill gaps|Resentment in host country; cultural myopia|
|**Polycentric**|Host country nationals manage local subsidiaries; parent company stays at HQ|Cheaper; reduces cultural myopia; better local understanding|Limited career mobility for local managers; isolation|
|**Geocentric**|Best talent from anywhere in the world placed where needed|Strong global culture; not myopic|Expensive; complex immigration laws|

**Cultural myopia** = when people see their own way as the only way — a key risk of the ethnocentric approach.

**Real-world examples:**

- Ethnocentric worked for: Panasonic, Sony, Hitachi
- Ethnocentric failed for: Walmart in Germany and South Korea
- Geocentric success: IBM

## Comparative HRM — Key Influencing Factors

**1. Cultural differences**

- Individualistic cultures (e.g. USA) → performance-based rewards, individual development
- Collectivist cultures (e.g. India) → team-based rewards, group decision-making
- Example: Google adapts HR practices per country — more team focus in Japan, more individual innovation in the US

**2. Legal and regulatory environment**

- Each country has its own employment contracts, working hours, minimum wage, and benefits laws
- Example: Wipro complies with India's provident fund and employee state insurance requirements

**3. Economic conditions**

- Developing countries → focus on basic benefits and job security
- Developed countries → extensive benefits and career development
- Example: Starbucks offers health benefits and stock options in the US; competitive wages and basic healthcare in developing markets

**4. Social and political environment**

- Social norms, political stability, and government policies all shape HR practices
- Example: Nestlé offers generous parental leave and flexible work in France; prioritises safety and job security in unstable regions

## Job Analysis

A systematic process to gather, document, and analyse information about the components of a job. Informs recruitment, performance evaluation, training, and compensation.

### Two Key Outputs

**Job description** — outlines tasks, duties, responsibilities, and working conditions. Tells employees what is expected of them.

**Job specification** — outlines the qualifications, skills, knowledge, and abilities required. Used to match candidates to roles during recruitment.

### Five Steps in Job Analysis

1. **Identify the purpose and scope** — define why the analysis is needed and which roles are included
2. **Select data collection methods** — choose from interviews, questionnaires, observation, or document review
3. **Collect job information** — gather data on tasks, responsibilities, required skills, working conditions
4. **Analyse the data** — identify themes, patterns, KSAs (knowledge, skills, abilities), and physical/environmental demands
5. **Document the results** — create or update job descriptions and specifications

### Four Approaches

|Approach|How it works|Best for|
|---|---|---|
|**Observation**|HR directly watches employees perform tasks|Detailed, accurate task information|
|**Interview**|Structured conversations with employees, supervisors, and subject matter experts|In-depth exploration including subjective aspects|
|**Questionnaire**|Surveys with open or closed questions distributed to multiple stakeholders|Large-scale data collection across locations|
|**O*NET**|US Dept of Labor online database with standardised job descriptions and competency profiles|Benchmarking and supplementing other methods|

### Benefits of Job Analysis

- Foundation for strategic workforce planning
- Clarifies role expectations and aligns them to organisational goals
- Improves recruitment and candidate selection
- Informs training and development programs
- Provides benchmarks for performance management
- Supports fair and equitable compensation structures

## Talent Acquisition

Talent acquisition is the **strategic** process of identifying, attracting, selecting, and onboarding people who fit the organisation's short and long-term needs. It is broader than recruitment.

**Recruitment** = filling a specific vacancy **Talent acquisition** = ongoing, strategic pipeline-building including workforce planning, employer branding, and relationship management

### Why it matters

- Competitive advantage — top talent outperforms competitors
- Innovation and growth — talented people drive new ideas
- Cost efficiency — reducing turnover saves money
- Employer brand — a strong strategy attracts higher-quality candidates

### Key Components of a Talent Acquisition Strategy

**Workforce planning** — analyse current workforce, identify skill gaps, forecast future needs, develop action plans

**Employer branding** — define your Employee Value Proposition ([[Employee Value Proposition (EVP)|EVP]]), communicate it consistently across all platforms, engage current employees as brand ambassadors

**Sourcing strategies** — job boards (LinkedIn, Indeed), social media recruiting, employee referrals, recruitment agencies, campus recruiting, talent pools

**Selection process** — structured interviews, psychometric and skills assessments, panel interviews, cultural fit assessment, reference and background checks

**Onboarding** — pre-boarding before day one, orientation, training, mentorship pairing, feedback collection

### Key Metrics to Track

|Metric|What it measures|
|---|---|
|Time to fill|Average time to fill a position|
|Cost per hire|Total cost of hiring one employee|
|Quality of hire|Performance and retention rates of new hires|
|Candidate experience|Feedback from applicants on the hiring process|
|Source of hire|Which sourcing channels produce the best candidates|
|Diversity metrics|Representation in candidate pool and new hires|

### Future Trends in Talent Acquisition

- [[Artificial Intelligence (AI)|AI]] and automation in resume screening and decision-making
- Remote work and virtual onboarding
- Gig economy — managing freelance and contract workers
- Employee experience from recruitment through to retention
- Predictive analytics to forecast hiring needs

## Training and Development

**Training** = equipping employees with specific skills and knowledge for their current role **Development** = preparing employees for future roles and responsibilities

### Why it matters

- Improves employee performance and productivity
- Increases employee engagement and retention
- Builds organisational adaptability and capacity for innovation

### Three Strategies for Effective Training

1. **Conduct a needs assessment** — survey and interview employees to identify skill gaps before designing programs
2. **Use diverse training methods** — blended learning (online + workshops + peer learning) caters to different styles and improves retention
3. **Provide continuous learning** — ongoing opportunities via learning management systems, not just one-off events

### Learning Styles

|Style|How they learn best|Effective methods|
|---|---|---|
|**Visual** (≈65%)|Images, charts, diagrams, video|Slides, infographics, mind maps, interactive whiteboards|
|**Auditory** (≈30%)|Listening, verbal communication|Lectures, podcasts, group discussions, verbal instructions|
|**Kinesthetic** (≈5%)|Hands-on, physical activity|Role-play, simulations, hands-on exercises, field trips|

Most people benefit from a combination — multimodal training programs are most effective.

### The [[Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation and Evaluation (ADDIE)|ADDIE]] Model — Training Design Framework

|Stage|What happens|
|---|---|
|**Analysis**|Identify learning needs and objectives|
|**Design**|Develop a blueprint for the training program|
|**Development**|Create training materials and resources|
|**Implementation**|Deliver the program to participants|
|**Evaluation**|Assess effectiveness and make improvements|

### Pedagogy vs Andragogy

**Pedagogy** = teaching children — structured, instructor-led, teacher directs the learning. Still useful for adult compliance training or foundational knowledge.

**Andragogy** = teaching adults — self-directed, experience-based, learner sets their own goals. Best for leadership development, professional upskilling, and workshops with case studies and role-play.

### Learning Organisations

Characteristics: commitment to lifelong learning, shared vision, open communication, a culture that encourages experimentation, and strong knowledge-sharing across teams. Examples: Google, Pixar.

## HR Excellence Framework

HR excellence = aligning the HR function with business objectives to build a workforce that drives productivity, engagement, and long-term success.

_"Organisations with excellent HR frameworks are 50% more likely to outperform their competitors."_

### Six Pillars

1. **Strategic workforce planning** — predict future talent needs, identify skills gaps, ensure the right people are in the right roles at the right time
2. **Employee experience and engagement** — flexible work, strong culture, mental health support, continuous feedback
3. **Leadership development and succession planning** — identify and nurture high-potential employees; mentorship and coaching
4. **Performance management and continuous learning** — replace annual reviews with real-time feedback; invest in ongoing development
5. **Diversity, equity, and inclusion ([[Diversity, Equity and Inclusion (DEI)|DEI]])** — unbiased hiring, inclusive workplace, transparent reporting
6. **Digital HR transformation** — AI-driven analytics, automated onboarding, HR chatbots, AI-assisted recruitment

### Four-Step Process for HR Excellence

1. **Market analysis** — assess salary benchmarks, workforce availability, skill gaps, and competitor positioning
2. **Legal considerations** — ensure compliance with employment contracts, taxation, anti-discrimination laws, and union regulations
3. **Localised HR strategy** — adapt to cultural values, economic conditions, and regional expectations (one-size-fits-all doesn't work globally)
4. **Implementation** — execute, track [[Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)|KPIs]] (retention rates, engagement scores, productivity), use HR analytics to continuously refine

## HR Legal Considerations

### Key Legal Areas

- **Employment laws** — e.g. minimum wage, overtime pay (US: Fair Labor Standards Act)
- **Anti-discrimination laws** — inclusive hiring language, unconscious bias training for hiring managers
- **Workplace safety** — e.g. OSHA regulations in manufacturing; regular safety training and audits

### Compliance Challenges

- Keeping up with constantly evolving laws
- Navigating conflicting regulations across regions and countries
- Balancing legal obligations with organisational culture

## Employee Benefits

Benefits are tools to attract, retain, and motivate talent — not just perks.

**Common types:** health insurance, retirement plans, paid time off ([[Paid Time Off (PTO)|PTO]])

**Innovative approaches:** flexible working hours, financial wellness programs, on-site childcare or elder care, tele-medicine, mental health counselling

**Challenges:** rising healthcare costs, customising for a diverse workforce, communicating benefits clearly

## Outsourcing HR Functions — Pros and Cons

|Pros|Cons|
|---|---|
|Cost savings — eliminates salaries, training, and HR technology costs|Loss of control — external teams may not understand your culture|
|Access to expertise and latest HR practices|Data security risks — sharing sensitive employee data with third parties|
|Frees leadership to focus on core business|Employee dissatisfaction — staff may feel disconnected from external HR|
|Better compliance and risk management|Hidden costs and vendor dependence over time|

## Business Structures for Global Expansion

|Structure|What it is|Best for|Challenges|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Subsidiary**|Separate legal entity owned by parent company|Deep market integration; tax optimisation; local credibility|Expensive to set up; complex compliance; cross-border management|
|**Representative office**|Local presence for research and liaison only — cannot generate revenue|Low-cost market exploration; brand visibility|Cannot sell or generate profit; limited operations|
|**Employer of Record ([[Employer of Record (EOR)|EOR]])**|Third party takes on legal employment responsibilities in a foreign country|Fast market entry; reduced legal risk; testing new markets|Less control; ongoing cost; dependence on provider|
|**Independent contractors**|Self-employed professionals hired for specific projects|Flexibility; access to niche skills; no benefits obligations|Misclassification risk; lack of loyalty; varying local compliance rules|

## Brown Bag Meetings

An informal training or discussion session — typically held at lunchtime — where employees share knowledge, learn new skills, or discuss projects without interrupting core work time.

The name comes from the old habit of bringing homemade lunches to work in brown paper bags.

### Four Types

|Type|Format|Best for|
|---|---|---|
|**Seminar**|One presenter shares knowledge, Q&A at end|Expertise sharing, tool training, department updates|
|**Small group**|Roundtable, interactive discussion (5–10 people)|Strategy sessions, peer learning, retrospectives|
|**Combination**|Mini presentation + small group discussion|Training with hands-on practice|
|**Social**|Casual conversation, no formal content|Team bonding, culture building, remote team connection|

### Benefits

- Low cost, high impact
- Builds a continuous learning culture
- Breaks down silos between departments
- Empowers any employee to lead a session
- Builds confidence and communication skills, especially in junior staff

### How to Run One

1. Pick the type and topic
2. Schedule it (lunchtime is ideal) and book the space or virtual room
3. Plan the content — keep it to 30 minutes, focus on engagement over slides
4. Promote it via Slack, email, or internal channels
5. Make it interactive — questions, live polls, breakout groups
6. Collect feedback and improve next time

**Common mistakes:** making it too formal, not encouraging discussion, choosing irrelevant topics, poor scheduling, forgetting to follow up.
