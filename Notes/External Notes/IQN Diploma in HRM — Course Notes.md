## MODULE 1 — Foundations of [[Human Resource Management (HRM)|HRM]]

### What is HRM?

Human Resource Management is the planned management of people in an organisation. It covers hiring, developing, rewarding, and retaining employees through [[Human Resources (HR)|HR]] policies, practices, and strategies. Every organisation — regardless of size — must manage people well to prosper.

Core HR functions:

- Recruitment and selection
    
- Employment contracts and job conditions
    
- Equal opportunity and anti-discrimination
    
- Disciplinary processes
    
- Reward and motivation
    
- Learning and development
    

What shapes HR in any organisation:

- Owner/senior management's HR philosophy
    
- Size (small orgs may have no HR dept — the owner does it all)
    
- Industry and operational context
    

---

### Personnel Management vs HRM

Personnel Management (PM): Reactive, administrative, day-to-day focus on staffing, pay, and movement of staff.

Human Resource Management (HRM): Strategic, proactive, long-term. Treats people as a competitive advantage. Emphasises trust, commitment, involvement, and collaboration.

Key distinction: PM reacts to people issues; HRM plans ahead and aligns people management with organisational goals.

| Reactive ←────────────→ Proactive |                 |
| --------------------------------- | --------------- |
| Personnel Mgmt                    | HRM             |
| Admin focus                       | Strategic focus |
| Short-term                        | Long-term       |
| Control                           | Commitment      |
| Processes                         | People          |

---

### History of HRM — Four Phases

|Phase|Era|Key development|
|---|---|---|
|Welfare Movement|19th century|Industrial Revolution; employers created "welfare officers" to deal with poor working conditions|
|Scientific Management|1880s–1920s|Frederick Taylor; focus on labour productivity; trade unions rise; "labour manager" roles emerge|
|Behavioural Science|1930s–1970s|Hawthorne studies (Elton Mayo); working conditions affect motivation; "Personnel Officer" role and PM become common|
|HRM Established|1980s–present|Recession drives productivity focus; tech growth; service sector expands; trade unions decline; HRM becomes a formal business function|

---

### Strategic HRM ([[Society for Human Resource Management (SHRM)|SHRM]])

Traditional HRM criticism: it doesn't visibly add business value the way sales or finance does.

SHRM fixes this by: connecting HR practices directly to business outcomes.

- Traditional HRM = micro level (policies, admin)
    
- SHRM = macro level (aligning HR to strategy and solving business problems)
    

Example: If a sales team is underperforming, SHRM means HR investigates root causes and coordinates a solution — not just processes paperwork.

### HRM and Business Context

HR is shaped by internal and external factors:

Internal: Organisation size, ownership type (private vs government), age/maturity, senior management ideology, financial condition, workforce characteristics, organisational culture.

External: Market competition, technological change, labour market conditions, government legislation, ethical environment.

---

### Contemporary Features of HRM (Vertical & Horizontal Integration)

Key modern HRM features:

- HRM is explicitly linked to corporate strategy
    
- Focus is on employee commitment, not just compliance
    
- HR is no longer just HR's job — line managers share responsibility
    
- HRM has a unitarist frame: managers and employees share common purpose
    
- Soft approach: employees treated as valuable assets (not machines)
    
- Evidence-based: HR practices guided by data and critical thinking
    

Vertical Integration: Matching HR policies/practices with business strategy. (e.g. strategy is to grow sales 200% → HR practices attract, hire and retain the best sales team)

Horizontal Integration: Ensuring HR policies are consistent with each other. (e.g. you can't increase sales commission AND restrict paternity leave — you're sending contradictory messages)

### HR Practices and Organisational Outcomes ([[High-Performance Work System (HPWS)|HPWS]])

HR practices → individual performance → organisational capability → desired outcomes.

When HR achieves this chain, the organisation has a High Performance Work System (HPWS).

Debate: Should HR serve the organisation first, or employees first? Answer: Focus on common interests between both.

---

### Strategic Linkage Between HR and Business Strategy

|Model|Description|
|---|---|
|Separation|No link between HR and business strategy|
|Fit|Business strategy developed first, HR follows|
|Dialogue|HR strategy influences business strategy to some extent|
|Holistic|Both developed together|
|HR Driven|HR strategy drives the business strategy|

---

### HR Roles: Specialists, Generalists, Business Partners

HR Specialist: Expert in one area (e.g. payroll, recruitment, training, [[Human Resource Information System (HRIS)|HRIS]]).

HR Generalist: Broad knowledge across all HR areas — recruitment, benefits, training, ER, safety, etc.

HR Business Partner: Experienced HR professional who works directly with senior management. Focuses on strategy, not day-to-day. Proposes people-related strategies to help achieve business goals.

Shared Service Centre: Centralised unit that handles routine/transactional HR tasks (payroll, benefits admin, leave queries) for all locations, freeing HR professionals for strategic work.

---

### Key Competencies of HR Practitioners

HR practitioners work across four dimensions: individual, organisational, contextual, and strategic.

Five key competencies:

1. Credible activist — delivers on promises
    
2. Capability builder — identifies and develops people's capabilities
    
3. Change champion — responds to and drives change
    
4. Innovator and integrator — implements best HR practices
    
5. Technology proponent — promotes use of technology
    

---

### The Role of the Line Manager in HR

Day-to-day HR is devolved to line managers — they handle immediate people management in their own teams. The HR manager advises and supports when needed. A partnership approach between line managers and HR is key to success.

## MODULE 2 — Workforce Planning

### The Workforce Planning Cycle (5 Stages)

1. Stock Taking — assess external factors (economic conditions, employment laws, technology) and internal factors (workforce size, skills, performance, conditions)
    
2. Forecasting — examine current workforce skills/competencies; develop succession planning; predict future labour supply and demand
    
3. Developing Action Plans:
    

- Demand = Supply → no action needed
    
- Demand > Supply → labour shortage → recruit more, retrain current staff
    
- Demand < Supply → labour surplus → lay offs, early retirement, reduce hours
    

5. Implementation — act on the plan; monitor and adjust as needed
    
6. Assessment — evaluate whether plans achieved strategic goals; learn from successes and failures

## MODULE 3 — Talent Management & Recruitment

### Talent Management

Talent management = attracting, recruiting, engaging, and retaining employees who add strategic value.

Four categories of employees:

|Category|Description|
|---|---|
|Underperformers|Low potential, poor performance — often first to go in a reduction|
|Key Performers|Competent in current role, less suited for further development — need reassurance|
|Untapped Potentials|Have capability but underperforming — investigate why; provide training|
|High Potentials (Stars)|High performance AND high potential — rare; core of the talent pool|

Key points:

- Talented employees value environment and growth opportunity, not just financial rewards
    
- Organisations need both a talent development policy and individualised plans
    
- Talent ≠ performance rating (though they often correlate)
    

---

### Strategic Recruitment & Selection (5 Stages)

1. Business strategy
    
2. Workforce planning
    
3. Job analysis
    
4. Recruitment
    
5. Selection
    

---

### Job Analysis

Purpose: Identify the knowledge, skills, abilities, attitudes and behaviours needed to successfully perform a role.

Two outputs:

- Job Description: Tasks, responsibilities, and purpose of the role
    
- Person Specification: Qualifications, skills, attributes and experience required
    

Both include essential (mandatory) and desirable (nice to have) characteristics.

Organisations also use competency-based criteria — specific behavioural competencies for each role (e.g. communication, leadership, teamwork).

---

### Recruitment Methods

|Method|Key Points|
|---|---|
|Internal|Cost-effective; candidate knows the org; motivates existing staff; limits innovation and diversity|
|External|Brings fresh ideas and diversity; more costly; use recruitment consultants, employee referrals, graduate programs, job fairs|
|E-Recruitment|Online ads, LinkedIn, social media; fast and cheap; risk of too many applications; use [[Applicant Tracking System (ATS)|ATS]] software to filter|
|International|Used when skills aren't available locally; high costs (transport, visas, relocation)|

Formal vs Informal: Formal = official advertising; Informal = word of mouth, social channels.

---

### The Job Advertisement

Drawn from job description and person specification. Must follow AIDA:

- Attention — grab potential candidates' attention
    
- Interest — generate interest in the role
    
- Desire — make them want the job
    
- Action — prompt them to apply
    

Application methods: Application forms (standardised, easier to compare) vs CVs + cover letters.

Employer branding: Positive brand = more and better applicants. Negative brand = deters talent.

---

### Shortlisting

Sifting process to reduce applicants to a manageable list of suitable candidates.

Shortlisting Matrix: Scores candidates against essential and desirable criteria from the person spec. Essential criteria can be weighted more heavily.

ATS (Applicant Tracking System): Software that scans CVs/applications for keywords. Can also run online ability tests and personality questionnaires at screening stage.

---

### Selection Methods

Key concepts:

- Reliability: Method consistently measures what it sets out to measure
    
- Validity: How accurately the method measures what it's supposed to measure
    
- Predictive validity: How well the method predicts future job performance
    

|Method|Notes|
|---|---|
|Interviews|Most popular. Types: telephone, video, panel, one-to-one. Can be structured or unstructured. Forms: competency-based, situational, strengths-based|
|Psychometric Testing|General intelligence, attainment, cognitive ability tests|
|Personality Profiling|Big Five (OCEAN), OPQ32|
|Work Sample Tests|High predictive validity; candidate performs actual job tasks|
|Assessment Centres|Multi-day; multiple assessors; uses combination of methods including leaderless group discussions|
|Graphology|Handwriting analysis; popular in Europe|

  

Common interview biases:

- Confirmatory bias (first impression error)
    
- Halo/horns effect
    
- Stereotyping
    
- Contrast error
    
- Projection error
    
- Attractiveness bias
    

---

### Making the Final Selection Decision

- If multiple selection methods used, assign weightings to each
    
- Use a scoring matrix to compare all candidates
    
- Check references and verify qualifications
    
- Ensure process was fair, consistent, documented, and free from discrimination
    
- Contact unsuccessful candidates respectfully
    
- Successful candidate signs employment contract; monitor performance post-hire to evaluate recruitment effectiveness

## MODULE 4 — Engagement, Induction & Turnover

### Employee Engagement

Work Engagement = physical + psychological + emotional connection to work:

- Vigour — high energy, mental resilience
    
- Absorption — full concentration, engrossed in work
    
- Dedication — strong involvement, pride, enthusiasm
    

Employee Engagement = Work engagement + connection to the organisation (through management support, rewards, autonomy, and values).

Benefits of engagement: Higher performance, more initiative, innovation, lower absenteeism, lower turnover, higher retention.

---

### Retention

Keeping good employees is a strategic challenge. Money matters — but once pay is competitive, other factors dominate: development, environment, culture, growth opportunity.

Retention strategy starts at recruitment — select for values alignment and organisational fit from the beginning.

---

### Induction vs Onboarding vs Orientation

- Orientation: A specific training event or course for new employees
    
- Induction: The broader process of adjusting to a new role and environment
    
- Onboarding: Getting new employees to feel they belong and contribute as quickly as possible
    

---

### The Survival Curve (3 Phases)

New employees are at highest risk of leaving in the first 6 weeks.

1. Induction Crisis — highest risk; new employee may find role isn't what they expected; values misalignment; stress/burnout
    
2. Differential Transit — employee starts to settle; risk decreases; typically first few months
    
3. Settled Connection — fully socialised; committed; dedicated; unlikely to leave
    

Goal: Move new employees to Settled Connection as quickly as possible.

Turnover Risk

  | \  
  |  \__  
  |     \______  
  |

Peak risk = first 6 weeks

---

### Induction Approaches

|Approach|Description|
|---|---|
|Traditional/Informational|Formal welcome, team intro, tour, admin overview. Employee left to find their own way.|
|Relational|Focuses on quickly building relationships so new employee knows who to ask and feels part of the team|

  

Best practice: Combine both. Line manager must be actively involved — induction is NOT solely HR's responsibility.

Onboarding schedule (two stages):

- Stage 1 (Informational): Mission, structure, job description, pay, H&S
    
- Stage 2 (Relational): Meet teams, understand objectives, meet key people, buddy/mentor introduction
    

---

### Socialisation Tools

- Mentor: More experienced employee; formal; development-focused; shares knowledge, culture, history
    
- Buddy: Informal; similar level to new employee; answers basic day-to-day questions
    

---

### Turnover

Pull factors: Outside the organisation's control (e.g. employee moves city). Organisation may offer flexible working or invite return.

Push factors: Within the organisation's control (dissatisfaction, lack of career progression, poor management). Use engagement surveys to identify causes.

Flexible benefits: Employees choose from a selection of reward options — reduces unwanted turnover.

Labour market impact:

- Economic boom → high turnover; organisations compete on salary/benefits to retain
    
- Economic downturn → focus on downsizing; survivor guilt; increased workload on remaining staff
    

Benefits of some turnover: Prevents groupthink; brings fresh ideas; removes underperformers.

Drawbacks: Cost and productivity loss during transition periods.

Measuring turnover: (Number of leavers ÷ Average number of employees) × 100. Must use consistent definitions across departments.

Cohort analysis: Analyse turnover by group (department, role type, hire date) to find patterns and target interventions.

## MODULE 5 — Performance Management

### Motivation Theories

Goal Setting Theory: Specific, challenging, and attainable goals motivate higher performance. Goals should be [[Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant and Time-Bound (SMART)|SMART]] (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Timed).

Expectancy Theory (VIE):

- E (Expectancy) — will effort lead to performance?
    
- I (Instrumentality) — will performance lead to reward?
    
- V (Valence) — how much do I value the reward?
    
- Motivation = E × I × V
    

Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence

If ANY = 0 → Motivation = 0

Equity Theory: Individuals compare their output/input ratio to others. If unequal, they adjust effort to rebalance.

---

### Engagement and Performance Management

A well-designed PM system enhances engagement. Three dimensions of fairness:

- Procedural Justice — is the process transparent and fair?
    
- Distributive Justice — are outcomes (ratings, rewards) fair?
    
- Interactional Justice — is the employee treated with respect throughout?
    

---

### Performance Appraisal Methods

|Method|Description|
|---|---|
|Rating (BARS)|Scale 1–5; specific behaviours anchored to each rating level|
|Ranking|Employees compared to each other (best to worst); forced ranking uses bell curve|
|Critical Incident|Appraiser records specific good/bad performance incidents over time|
|Upward Appraisal (180°)|Employees rate their manager; usually anonymous|
|360° Feedback|Ratings from peers, subordinates, supervisors, sometimes customers|
|Competency-Based|Evaluates actual behaviour against defined competency list|

  

---

### Performance Appraisal Biases

|Bias|Description|
|---|---|
|Contrast Effect|Rated against previous candidate rather than own goals|
|First Impression Error|Overall assessment based only on early performance|
|Recency Effect|Only recent performance considered; early performance ignored|
|Halo Effect|One negative trait affects overall rating|
|Similar-to-Me Effect|Favourable rating because candidate shares appraiser's characteristics|
|Central Tendency Bias|Rates everyone as average; avoids tough decisions|
|Skewing Bias|Overly positive or negative across the board|
|Attributional Bias|Assumes poor performance is due to laziness without investigating real cause|
|Stereotyping|Generalises based on group membership|

  

---

### Overcoming Performance Management Pitfalls

1. Train appraisers adequately
    
2. Involve both appraiser and appraisee in process design
    
3. Ensure senior management openly supports the system
    
4. Keep administration simple
    
5. Always follow up on appraisal decisions and actions
    

Innovative alternatives:

- OKR (Objectives and Key Results): Set objectives + measurable key results; yes/no accountability
    
- Feed Forward Interview: Employee identifies positive experiences and strengths; reflects on what conditions enabled them; identifies own areas for improvement without external judgment
    

---

### Managing Underperformance (5 Steps)

1. Identify and agree on the problem
    
2. Establish the reasons for underperformance
    
3. Decide and agree on action required
    
4. Provide support and resources
    
5. Monitor and provide regular feedback
    

Solutions include: SMART goal-setting, coaching ([[Goal, Reality, Options and Will (GROW)|GROW]] model), training and development, job rotation.

GROW Model: Goal → Reality → Options → Will (What will you do?)

## MODULE 6 — Reward

### Reward System

A combination of financial and non-financial elements to compensate employees.

Core objectives: Align with org goals; attract and retain; motivate performance; integrate with other HR policies; comply with employment laws.

---

### Financial Rewards

Direct Pay:

- Base pay (hourly/weekly/monthly)
    
- Overtime
    
- Premium pay (weekends, night shifts)
    
- Performance Related Pay ([[Performance-Related Pay (PRP)|PRP]])
    

Indirect Pay:

- Statutory benefits (legally required): holiday pay, maternity/paternity leave, disability benefits
    
- Organisation-specific benefits (voluntary): pension contributions, EAP, company car, subsidised meals, on-site childcare
    

---

### Types of Performance Related Pay (PRP)

|Type|Description|
|---|---|
|Merit Pay|Permanent addition to base pay for achieving goals|
|Piecework|Pay per unit produced|
|Commission|Percentage of sales; increases with volume|
|Bonuses|Variable pay for hitting targets; re-earned each period|
|Team-Based Pay|Linked to team performance|
|Profit Sharing|Employees share in company profits|
|Employee Share Ownership (ESOP)|Employees receive company shares|
|Gainsharing|Employees share in efficiency savings (e.g. waste reduction)|

  

---

### Non-Financial Rewards

- Job security — especially valued in volatile environments
    
- Career development — planned progression and growth
    
- Work-life balance policies — flexible hours, working from home
    
- Recognition — informal (word of thanks) or formal (certificates, awards); cheap but highly effective
    

---

### Pay Structure: Internal Alignment and External Comparison

Internal Alignment: Comparing all jobs within the organisation to create a hierarchy; determines relative pay levels.

External Comparison: Comparing pay against competitors; ensures the org can attract and retain. Sources: government labour stats, salary surveys, job ads, employee intel.

Job Evaluation (for internal alignment): Establishes relative worth of jobs.

Non-Analytical Methods:

- Ranking — simplest; jobs ordered by importance
    
- Paired comparison — matrix comparing all job pairs
    
- Classification systems — jobs grouped into families/grades
    

Analytical Methods (Point Method):

1. Identify factors common to all jobs (e.g. experience, education, responsibility)
    
2. Define levels for each factor; assign points
    
3. Weight factors by importance
    
4. Calculate total weighted points per job
    
5. Group jobs with similar totals into pay grades

## MODULE 7 — Learning and Development

### Key Definitions

|Term|Description|
|---|---|
|Learning|Broadest concept — acquiring knowledge, skills, attitudes, behaviours|
|Training|Systematic, short-term, specific purpose|
|Development|Long-term, gradual growth; individual-led|
|Education|Formal, long-term, leads to qualifications|

  

---

### Strategic Roles of L&D

1. Attract and retain talent
    
2. Develop people capabilities
    
3. Build employer brand
    
4. Motivate and engage employees
    

---

### Learning Styles (Honey & Mumford)

- Activists — learn by doing; enthusiastic; act first, reflect later
    
- Reflectors — observe, collect data, reflect before concluding; shy
    
- Theorists — logical, step-by-step; analytical; perfectionists
    
- Pragmatists — test ideas in practice; seek practical application
    

Note: Styles are not exclusive — a person can display multiple styles.

---

### On-the-Job Training Methods

- Self-directed learning
    
- Induction training
    
- Planned learning experience (rotation through departments)
    
- Coaching (one-to-one; performance-driven)
    
- Mentoring (holistic; development-driven)
    
- E-learning / [[Learning Management System (LMS)|LMS]]
    

Coaching = performance-driven. Mentoring = development-driven.

---

### Off-the-Job Training Methods

- Classroom lectures
    
- Audio-visual
    
- Vestibule training (using real equipment away from workplace)
    
- Case studies
    
- Role-playing
    
- Management games
    

---

### Planning and Delivering Training: [[Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation and Evaluation (ADDIE)|ADDIE]] Model

1. Analysis — identify learning needs; gap analysis (what do people know vs what they should know)
    
2. Design — subject matter, methods, pacing, materials; trainer-centred vs trainee-centred
    
3. Development — detailed session plans, content, learning outcomes, visual aids
    
4. Implementation — delivery; logistics; assessment
    
5. Evaluation — Kirkpatrick's four levels:
    

- Level 1: Reaction (how did participants feel?)
    
- Level 2: Learning (did they actually learn?)
    
- Level 3: Behaviour (are they using it on the job?)
    
- Level 4: Results (did it benefit the organisation?)
    

Analyse → Design → Develop → Implement → Evaluate ↺

## MODULE 8 — Career Development

Career development = lifelong process of managing learning, work, leisure, and transitions.

Three components:

1. Learning — education, training, credentials
    
2. Capability — raw skills (data management, strategy, project management, software, leadership)
    
3. Experience — chain of positions around a career theme; includes volunteering and internships
    

Developing career opportunities: Identify career paths through job analysis; communicate available vacancies and requirements to employees; align org career opportunities with individual aspirations; provide career counselling.

## MODULE 9 — Employee Relations

### Introduction

Employee relations = relationship between employers and employees (individual and collective).

Four approaches:

1. Adversarial — org decides; employees either comply or refuse to cooperate
    
2. Traditional — reasonable relationship; employees react through trade union reps
    
3. Partnership — employees involved in developing and implementing policies
    
4. Power Sharing — employees involved in day-to-day and strategic decision-making
    

A climate of trust is essential for a positive employment relationship.

---

### The Psychological Contract

Unwritten expectations between employer and employee (distinct from the legal contract).

Employer expects: Competence, effort, compliance, commitment, loyalty.

Employee expects: Fairness, consistency, job security, training, growth opportunity.

A positive psychological contract leads to higher commitment, satisfaction, and better employee relations. Requires: transparency, ongoing dialogue, consensus over coercion.

Employer gives → Pay, security, growth

Employee gives → Effort, loyalty, performance

Break = disengagement

---

### Employee Voice

The means by which employees communicate views to their employer.

Benefits for employers: Trust, innovation, productivity, improvement. Benefits for employees: Feeling valued, job satisfaction, influence, development opportunities.

Channels: Individual (direct to management), collective (through trade union reps), formal (surveys, suggestion schemes), informal (team meetings).

Multiple channels needed — employees won't use voice if only one option exists.

---

### Industrial Relations and Collective Bargaining

Collective bargaining: Process through which trade unions negotiate with employers on wages, conditions, hours, training, [[Occupational Health and Safety (OHS)|OHS]], etc. Outcomes = collective agreements.

Negotiation approaches:

- Distributive bargaining — win-lose; dividing a fixed resource
    
- Integrative bargaining — win-win; both parties' needs explored and met as far as possible
    

Dispute resolution:

- Conciliation — third party brings parties together; does NOT make recommendations
    
- Mediation — third party suggests and persuades; does NOT impose a decision
    
- Arbitration — third party makes a binding recommendation

## MODULE 10 — Employee Welfare

### What is Welfare?

Services and benefits beyond standard pay that organisations provide to support employee wellbeing.

Welfare is considered a hygiene factor — its absence demotivates, but its presence doesn't necessarily motivate.

Modern approach: proactive, positive, holistic, wide-ranging — addresses physical, mental, and emotional wellbeing.

---

### Occupational Health

Concerned with the effects of the working environment on employees.

Environmental factors to manage: Noise, ventilation, temperature, lighting, space, cleanliness, sanitation, rest facilities.

Hazardous substances: Warning signs, training, secure storage, protective clothing.

Accident prevention: Safety awareness, consultation with staff and unions, training, safety inspections, compliance with laws.

Ergonomics: Scientific study of the relationship between people and their working environment; designs tools, furniture and processes to minimise physical strain.

---

### Stress and Work-Life Balance

Stress = tension from pressure, overwork, insecurity, failure. Some stress is useful; excessive stress (strain) is damaging.

Harmful stress symptoms: Irritability, sleeplessness, depression, substance abuse, physical illness.

Stress management techniques: Workplace counselling, rest breaks, relaxation techniques, exercise, delegation, planning, assertiveness.

---

### Workplace Counselling

Advising = directive; offering information and recommendations. Counselling = non-directive; helping others find their own solutions.

Counselling process (3 stages):

1. Review current scenario (identify and clarify problems)
    
2. Develop preferred scenario (identify goals)
    
3. Determine how to get there (develop action strategies)
    

Confidentiality is critical throughout.

## MODULE 11 — Equal Opportunities and Managing Diversity

### Equal Opportunities

Equal access and fair treatment regardless of gender, race, ethnicity, age, disability, or religious belief.

Four types of discrimination:

1. Direct — treating one group less favourably than another
    
2. Indirect — applying a rule that disadvantages one group more than another (even if applied equally)
    
3. Victimisation — disadvantaging someone because they exercised legal rights
    
4. Harassment — conduct that violates dignity or creates hostile/offensive environment
    

---

### Managing Diversity vs Equal Opportunity

|Dimension|Equal Opportunity|Managing Diversity|
|---|---|---|
|Purpose|Reduce discrimination|Utilise each person's potential|
|Argument|Moral/ethical|Business objectives|
|Focus|Groups|Individuals|
|Benefit|Improved opportunities for disadvantaged groups|Opportunities for all employees|
|Responsibility|HR managers|All managers|
|Focus area|Recruitment|Culture|
|Change required|Systems and practices|Corporate culture|

  

Equal opportunity = fair treatment. Managing diversity = recognising and respecting individual differences.

## MODULE 12 — Exit Management

### Overview

Exit management = the process of managing an employee's departure from the organisation.

Reasons for exit: Dismissal, resignation, mutual agreement, redundancy, frustration of contract (e.g. serious illness), end of contract, retirement.

Exit process: Paperwork and clearances; final settlement of dues; exit interview; respectful farewell.

Exit interview: Relaxed two-way conversation; gather feedback to improve the work environment.

---

### Redundancy

Reduction in employee numbers due to bad business circumstances.

Options before redundancy: Freeze recruitment, reduce overtime, reduce part-timers and temp staff, work sharing, temporary layoffs.

If redundancy is unavoidable: Consult affected employees; give reasons for selection; provide redundancy payments; consider outplacement support.

---

### Disciplinary Process (4 Stages)

1. Informal discussion
    
2. First written warning
    
3. Final written warning
    
4. Dismissal
    

All written warnings and dismissal must be approved by higher authority. Facts should be recorded in writing throughout.

---

### Retirement

- Flexible retirement: Minimum age but fit employees can continue
    
- Fixed retirement: Enforced at a set age; no exceptions
    

Organisations need retirement policies covering: when employees retire, conditions for working beyond retirement age, pre-retirement training and advice.

---

### Resignation

Employee decision to leave. Reasons include: lack of opportunities, poor management, poor ethics, inadequate compensation, monotony, personal circumstances.

Resignations should be submitted in writing; organisations should have a clear process for handling them.

## MODULE 13 — HR Analytics

HR Analytics = collecting and analysing HR-related data to improve organisational performance. Also called people analytics, workforce analytics, or talent analytics.

Predictive HR Analytics: Uses past and present data to forecast future outcomes (e.g. identifying employees most likely to leave).

Example — Cost of turnover: When an employee leaves, productivity drops before they go, during transition, and while the new hire ramps up. Analytics can calculate the true cost of that gap — which is typically much higher than just the salary.

Example — Training effectiveness: By comparing promotion rates between a trained group and an untrained control group, HR can calculate the odds ratio to prove training [[Return on Investment (ROI)|ROI]] (e.g. "trained employees were 254% more likely to be promoted").

Data → Insight → Decision → Action → Business Impact

## MODULE 14 — HR Information Systems (HRIS)

HRIS = software that uses technology to manage HR functions.

Benefits: Record keeping, compliance, efficiency, informs HR strategy, enables employee self-service.

Key HRIS modules:

- Applicant Tracking System (ATS)
    
- Payroll
    
- Benefits administration
    
- Time and attendance
    
- Training/LMS
    
- Performance management
    
- Employee self-service
    
- Reporting and analytics
    

HRIS Implementation (6 Stages):

1. Search — select the right software provider
    
2. Plan and align — form implementation committee
    
3. Define and design — specify user groups and functional requirements
    
4. Configure and test — test and refine with user acceptance testing
    
5. Train and communicate — train staff; create FAQs and support materials
    
6. Deploy and sustain — go live; maintain and benefit

## MODULE 15 — HR Audit

HR Audit = evaluating HR policies, procedures, and practices to check if they are effective and compliant.

Can be conducted by internal HR specialists or external consultants. Auditors must be independent.

HR Audit Phases (3):

1. Pre-audit information — review HR manuals, policies, procedures, handbooks
    
2. Pre-audit self-assessment — questionnaire completed by relevant teams
    
3. On-site audit — interviews with employees; in-depth review
    

Scope: Can audit compliance with employment laws, strategic alignment of HR, or be function-specific (e.g. auditing only the recruitment function or reward practices).

## MODULE 17 — International HRM

International HRM = employing and managing people in multinational or transnational organisations; working across national boundaries to formulate and implement strategies for an international workforce.

Factors influencing international HR decisions: Labour costs, skills availability, employment laws, industrial relations, culture, trade union power.

### Three Recruitment Options for Multinationals

|Strategy|Description|Pros|Cons|
|---|---|---|---|
|Parent country nationals|Transfer home country employees to key foreign roles|Transfers culture and practices|Alienates locals; expats may struggle to adapt|
|Local nationals|Hire from host country for key positions|Better local cooperation; avoids expatriate process|Less standardisation of parent culture|
|Best available regardless of nationality|Hire the best person globally|Genuine cross-cultural experience|Difficult; requires strong coordination|

### Convergence vs Divergence in International HRM

Convergence: One organisation, same HR practices everywhere.

- Argument: Core HR needs (attract, retain, motivate, develop) are universal; globalisation encourages shared practices.
    

Divergence: Different HR practices across different countries.

- Argument: Cultural differences, national legal frameworks, different education/training systems, different industrial relations traditions all demand different approaches.
    

Conclusion: Think global, act local — multinational companies need consistency in core activities and values, but flexibility in non-core areas.
