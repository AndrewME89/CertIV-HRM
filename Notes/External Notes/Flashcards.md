#flashcards/legislation

What is the **key purpose** of the Fair Work Act 2009?::To govern Australia's employee/employer relationship by providing a safety net of minimum entitlements ([[National Employment Standards]]), enabling flexible work and fairness at work, preventing discrimination, and protecting the right to engage in industrial activities. Administered by the [[Key Terms and Definitions#Fair Work Commission (FWC)|Fair Work Commission]].

<!--SR:!2026-03-30,1,236-->

What body administers the Fair Work Act?::The **Fair Work Commission (FWC)** — the national workplace relations tribunal. It handles unfair dismissal claims, enterprise agreement approvals, and the BOOT test. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Fair Work Commission (FWC)|Key Terms]].

<!--SR:!2026-03-30,1,236-->

How many NES entitlements are there?::11

<!--SR:!2026-04-02,4,276-->

List the **11 NES entitlements** (use the mnemonic **MAP CALL PFNS**)::Maximum weekly hours (38 + reasonable additional) · Annual leave (4 weeks; 5 for shift workers) · Personal/carer's leave (10 days) · Compassionate leave (2 days per occasion) · Parental leave (12 months unpaid) · Long service leave · Family & domestic violence leave · Public holidays · Flexible working arrangements requests · Notice of termination & redundancy pay · Statements (FWIS + CEIS). See [[National Employment Standards]].

<!--SR:!2026-03-30,1,236-->

Who do the NES apply to?::All employees in the **national workplace relations system**, regardless of their award, enterprise agreement or employment contract. Casual employees receive a **subset** only. See [[National Employment Standards]].

What is the **BOOT** test?::The **Better Off Overall Test** — applied by the Fair Work Commission to ensure employees are better off under an enterprise agreement than the relevant modern award. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Better Off Overall Test (BOOT)|Key Terms]].

<!--SR:!2026-04-01,3,256-->

What is a **Modern Award**?::A legally enforceable document setting minimum pay and employment conditions for a specific industry or occupation. Cannot provide less than the NES. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Modern Award|Key Terms]].

<!--SR:!2026-03-30,1,236-->

What is an **Enterprise Agreement**?::A collective agreement made between an employer and employees at the enterprise level, approved by the Fair Work Commission. Must pass the **BOOT**. Cannot undercut NES. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Enterprise Agreement (EA)|Key Terms]].

<!--SR:!2026-03-30,1,236-->

What is **unfair dismissal**?::Termination that is harsh, unjust, or unreasonable under the Fair Work Act. Managed by the Fair Work Commission. Contrast with **[[Key Terms and Definitions#Redundancy|redundancy]]** (role no longer needed). See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Unfair Dismissal|Key Terms]].

<!--SR:!2026-03-30,1,236-->

What are **general protections**?::Provisions in the Fair Work Act that protect employees from **adverse action** (e.g. dismissal, demotion) for exercising a workplace right, engaging in union activity, or having/not having a physical attribute. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#General Protections|Key Terms]].

<!--SR:!2026-03-30,1,236-->

What is the purpose of the **Racial Discrimination Act 1975**?::Protects people from discrimination based on race, colour, or national or ethnic origin. Applies to recruitment, workplace conduct, and all HR policies. See [[Key Legislation & Regulations#Racial Discrimination Act 1975|Key Legislation]].

What is the purpose of the **Sex Discrimination Act 1984**?::Protects against discrimination based on sex, gender identity, intersex status, sexual orientation, marital/relationship status, family responsibilities, pregnancy, and breastfeeding. See [[Key Legislation & Regulations#Sex Discrimination Act 1984|Key Legislation]].

What is the purpose of the **Age Discrimination Act 2004**?::Prohibits age-based discrimination in employment, education, accommodation, and services — protects **both** young and older people. See [[Key Legislation & Regulations#Age Discrimination Act 2004|Key Legislation]].

What is the purpose of the **Disability Discrimination Act 1992**?::Prohibits discrimination against people with disabilities in employment, education, access to premises, accommodation, sport, goods, services, and facilities. See [[Key Legislation & Regulations#Disability Discrimination Act 1992|Key Legislation]].

What is the purpose of the **Work Health and Safety Act 2011**?::Protects the health, safety and welfare of all workers and others in workplaces. Requires risk assessments, hazard identification, and safe work practices. See [[Key Legislation & Regulations#Work Health and Safety Act 2011|Key Legislation]].

What is the **Privacy Act 1988** and why does it matter to HR?::Governs collection, use, storage, and disclosure of personal information. HR must obtain consent before collecting personal data, store it securely, and only use appropriate information in decisions. The Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) apply. See [[01_Ethical Requirements]] and [[Key Legislation & Regulations#Privacy Act 1988|Key Legislation]].

What employment instruments **cannot undercut** the NES?::Awards, enterprise agreements, and employment contracts. The NES sets the **minimum floor** — nothing can provide less. See [[National Employment Standards]].

#flashcards/hr-strategy

What is **strategic planning** in HR?::Setting major organisational objectives and developing comprehensive plans to achieve them — including structure, strategies, policies, and how to survive and prosper long-term. See [[01_Human Resources Strategy#Strategic Planning|Human Resources Strategy]].

What is **operational planning** in HR?::Translating the strategic plan into detailed day-to-day tasks for running the organisation. Defines specific activities, timelines, and resources needed. See [[01_Human Resources Strategy#Operational Planning|Human Resources Strategy]].

What is the relationship between **HR strategy** and **business strategy**?::The HR strategy must **align with** the organisation's business plans, objectives and activities — it sets direction for all key HR functions (recruitment, performance, training, etc.) to help achieve business goals. See [[01_Human Resources Strategy]].

What does **employer of choice** mean in HR?::A prestigious recognition meaning the organisation attracts and retains the best available talent through superior employment conditions, culture, remuneration, and reputation. A key HR strategy goal. See [[01_Human Resources Strategy]].

What are the three levels of **Strategy → Goals → Tasks**?::Strategy = high-level direction · Goals = actionable objectives that deliver the strategy · Tasks = specific actions that break down each goal. See [[01_Human Resources Strategy#Overview of Strategy, Goals, Actions|Human Resources Strategy]].

What is **Strategic HRM (SHRM)**?::Moving HR from administrative/micro-level (policies, admin) to macro-level — directly connecting HR practices to business outcomes and solving business problems through people strategy. See [[IQN Diploma in HRM — Course Notes#Strategic HRM (SHRM)|IQN Course Notes]].

What is a **Human Resources Manager's** role? (3 key responsibilities)::1. Develops and implements HR policies and procedures · 2. Provides guidance to executives on strategic planning · 3. Manages workforce planning and succession planning. See [[01_Human Resources Strategy]].

What is a **Recruitment Officer's** role? (3 key responsibilities)::1. Organises and conducts recruitment and selection · 2. Supports the onboarding process for new hires · 3. Conducts exit interviews with departing employees. See [[01_Human Resources Strategy]].

What is an **Employee Relations Officer's** role? (3 key responsibilities)::1. Negotiates terms and conditions of employment · 2. Manages and mediates complaints, conflicts, and grievances · 3. Participates in employee relations activities such as wrongful dismissal claims. See [[01_Human Resources Strategy]].

What is **succession planning**?::The process of identifying and developing employees to fill key roles in the future. Relies on workforce data to identify gaps. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Succession Planning|Key Terms]] and [[03_Collate and Analyse Data]].

What is an **Employee Value Proposition (EVP)**?::The unique benefits and opportunities an employer offers to attract and retain employees. Central to being an employer of choice. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Employee Value Proposition (EVP)|Key Terms]].

#flashcards/policies

What is the difference between a **policy** and a **procedure**?::A **policy** sets guidelines, tone, and values — it's the "what and why". A **procedure** provides step-by-step instructions to comply with the policy — it's the "how". See [[01_Policies and Procedures]].

Name **five** HR-specific policies commonly found in organisations.::Recruitment & selection policy · Anti-discrimination policy · Grievance handling policy · Performance management policy · Training & development policy. See [[01_Policies and Procedures]] for full table.

Name **five** organisational (non-HR) policies.::Privacy policy · Code of conduct · Work health and safety policy · Drug and alcohol policy · Social media policy. See [[01_Policies and Procedures]].

Why must HR policies be regularly reviewed?::Outdated policies risk non-compliance with legislation, may contradict newer laws, and may not reflect current technology or best practices. Reviews keep policies aligned with business strategy and legally compliant. See [[02_Reviewing Policies and Procedures]].

What are **four questions** from a policy audit review checklist?::Is this policy up-to-date? · Has the policy been reviewed? · Does it comply with current legislation? · Is it consistent with the mission and strategic plan of the organisation? See [[02_Reviewing Policies and Procedures]].

What legislation must HR policies comply with? (5 key Acts)::Fair Work Act 2009 · Work Health & Safety Act 2011 · Privacy Act 1988 · Anti-discrimination legislation (Racial, Sex, Age, Disability Discrimination Acts) · National Employment Standards. See [[Key Legislation & Regulations]] and [[02_Reviewing Policies and Procedures]].

What is a **Code of Conduct**?::A set of rules outlining expected behaviours, ethical standards, and responsibilities for all employees. It establishes the moral and ethical requirements employees must follow. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Code of Conduct|Key Terms]] and [[02_Working Ethically]].

#flashcards/ethics

What are **three ethical requirements** associated with HR functions?::1. Privacy — apply Privacy Act 1988 when collecting/storing personal data · 2. Equal treatment — EEO and non-discrimination in recruitment, interviews, and performance management · 3. Accuracy — only use verified, accurate information in employment decisions. See [[01_Ethical Requirements]].

What **act** governs privacy obligations in HR?::The **Privacy Act 1988** — HR must obtain consent, store data securely, and only use appropriate information. The Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) apply. See [[Key Legislation & Regulations#Privacy Act 1988|Key Legislation]].

Name **four ethical behaviours** in the workplace.::Punctuality · Following the Code of Conduct · Respecting colleagues and clients · Exercising accountability (taking responsibility for your actions). See [[02_Working Ethically]].

Why is it important to provide ethics information to employees?::Employees need to know the organisation's values, code of conduct, policies, and obligations _before and during_ employment to perform their duties and meet required standards. It builds trust and reduces misconduct risk. See [[02_Working Ethically]].

What is **EEO** (Equal Employment Opportunity)?::The principle that employment decisions must be based on **merit** without discrimination. Supported by anti-discrimination legislation. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Equal Employment Opportunity (EEO)|Key Terms]].

What is the HR obligation around **security and storage** of personal information?::Personal information must be securely stored and protected from unauthorised access, use, or disclosure — safeguarding employees' privacy and maintaining trust. Governed by Privacy Act 1988 and managed through HRIS systems. See [[01_Ethical Requirements]] and [[01_Technology to Collect Data and Information]].

#flashcards/data-and-technology

What is an **HRIS / HRMS**?::A **Human Resource Information System** (or Management System) — software used to manage employee data, payroll, and HR processes. Centralises and automates key HR functions. See [[01_Technology to Collect Data and Information]] and [[Key Terms and Definitions#Human Resources Information System (HRIS)|Key Terms]].

Name **four types of data** stored in a HRMS.::Recruitment records (job descriptions, applications, interview records) · Employee personal details and profiles · Performance management data (reviews, KPIs) · Time and absence management (timesheets, leave, payroll). See [[01_Technology to Collect Data and Information]].

What is an **ATS**?::An **Applicant Tracking System** — software used to manage recruitment processes including job postings, applications, and candidate tracking. Often a module within the HRMS. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Applicant Tracking System (ATS)|Key Terms]].

What is an **LMS**?::A **Learning Management System** — software used to deliver, track, and manage employee training and development programs. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Learning Management System (LMS)|Key Terms]].

What are **internal** sources of workforce data? (5 examples)::Vacancy data · Recruitment data · Absenteeism rates · Performance reviews · Employee engagement survey results. See [[03_Human Resource Data and Information]] for full list.

What are **external** sources of workforce data? (4 examples)::Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) · Relevant legislation and regulations · Industry surveys · Labour market data (e.g. Jobs and Skills Australia dashboards). See [[02_Identify Sources of Workforce Data]].

What is the difference between **quantitative** and **qualitative** HR data?::Quantitative = numeric data (headcount, turnover %, KPIs, absenteeism rates) — presented as graphs/charts/tables. Qualitative = explores the "why" behind the numbers (surveys, focus groups, interviews). Both are used together for full insight. See [[02_How to Use Data to Document Key Findings]].

What are **HR metrics**?::Data used to measure HR effectiveness — e.g. turnover rate, absenteeism, time-to-hire, training completion, engagement scores. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)|KPIs]] and [[03_Collate and Analyse Data]].

What is **turnover rate**?::The percentage of employees leaving an organisation over a given period. A key workforce sustainability metric. See [[03_Sustainability Issues#^fe49c1]].

What valuable information can HR analytics provide? (4 examples)::Establish key labour market trends · Identify current and future skills shortages · Estimate future staffing needs · Identify training gaps and succession planning needs. See [[03_Collate and Analyse Data]].

#flashcards/sustainability

Define **organisational sustainability**.::The ability of the organisation to sustain its operations under changing markets, maintain success over time, and provide a productive, harmonious, stable, and resilient organisation that supports present and future generations. See [[03_Sustainability Issues#^ae91fa]].

Define **workforce sustainability**.::An organisation's capacity to maintain a capable, engaged, and motivated workforce over time — encompassing staff retention, succession planning, workforce planning, sustainable leadership, developing employee competencies, managing employee relations, and ensuring the workforce can meet present and future organisational needs. See [[03_Sustainability Issues#^fe49c1]].

What are the **three types** of sustainability issues HR deals with?::1. Financial sustainability · 2. Workforce sustainability (retention, planning, competencies, leadership) · 3. Social sustainability (Corporate Social Responsibility, DEI). See [[03_Sustainability Issues]].

What is **social sustainability** in the workplace context?::Ensuring the organisation acts responsibly toward people — including diversity and inclusion, equitable treatment, community engagement, and ethical practices. Related to DEI and the CBSA Access, Equity & Anti-Discrimination Policy. See [[03_Sustainability Issues]].

Why is **diversity and inclusion** a sustainability issue?::Poor D&I leads to higher turnover, lower engagement, reduced innovation, and reputational damage. Effective D&I builds a workforce that reflects the community, improves decision-making, and supports long-term organisational viability. See [[03_Sustainability Issues]].

#flashcards/recruitment-and-talent

What is the difference between **Talent Acquisition** and **Recruitment**?::Talent Acquisition is **strategic and long-term** — builds talent pipelines, focuses on future needs, includes employer branding. Recruitment is **reactive and short-term** — fills immediate vacancies. Recruitment is a _subset_ of Talent Acquisition. See [[Talent Acquisition — HR Study Notes]] and [[Key Terms and Definitions#Talent Acquisition (TA)|Key Terms]].

What is **job analysis**?::The systematic process of collecting, analysing, and documenting information about a job. It's the foundation of recruitment, training, performance management, and compensation. See [[Job Analysis — HR Study Notes]].

What is the difference between a **Job Description (JD)** and a **Position Description (PD)**?::A JD outlines duties, responsibilities, and requirements of the role itself. A PD is more formal and also includes reporting relationships and how the role fits within the organisation. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Job Description (JD)|Key Terms]] and [[Key Terms and Definitions#Position Description (PD)|Key Terms]].

What is **onboarding**?::The process of integrating a new employee into the organisation and preparing them for their role — includes induction, training, policy access, and ongoing check-ins. Ethical obligations apply. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Onboarding|Key Terms]] and [[01_Ethical Requirements]].

What is a **probation period**?::An initial period of employment during which an employee's suitability for the role is assessed. Managed in HRIS. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Probation Period|Key Terms]].

#flashcards/performance-and-er

What is **performance management**?::A continuous process of setting expectations, monitoring performance, and providing feedback — aligned with KPIs and legal requirements (non-discrimination, privacy). See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Performance Management|Key Terms]].

What is a **PIP** (Performance Improvement Plan)?::A formal plan designed to address and improve an employee's underperformance. Used after informal feedback has failed. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)|Key Terms]].

What is a **SMART goal**?::Specific · Measurable · Achievable · Relevant · Time-bound. Used in performance planning.

What is **misconduct** vs **serious misconduct**?::Misconduct = behaviour that breaches workplace policies or standards (formal warning process). Serious misconduct = severe behaviour (e.g. theft, fraud, violence) that may justify **immediate dismissal**. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Misconduct|Key Terms]] and [[Key Terms and Definitions#Serious Misconduct|Key Terms]].

What is a **grievance procedure**?::A formal process through which employees can raise and resolve workplace complaints. HR must follow it consistently and fairly. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Grievance Procedure|Key Terms]] and [[02_Working Ethically]].

What is **natural justice / procedural fairness** in HR?::The right to be heard before a decision is made. In disciplinary processes, this means the employee must know the allegations, be given opportunity to respond, and have the decision made impartially.

What is **redundancy**?::The termination of employment when a role is no longer required by the organisation. Notice and redundancy pay entitlements are set by the [[National Employment Standards]]. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Redundancy|Key Terms]].

What is an **exit interview**?::A structured discussion with a departing employee to gather feedback on their experience. Provides qualitative workforce data. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Exit Interview|Key Terms]].

#flashcards/whs

What is a **hazard**?::A potential source of harm or risk in the workplace. Must be identified and assessed under the WHS Act 2011. See [[Key Terms and Definitions#Hazard|Key Terms]].

What is the **hierarchy of controls** for WHS?::Elimination → Substitution → Engineering controls → Administrative controls → Personal Protective Equipment (PPE). Most effective to least effective.

What is **duty of care** in WHS?::The legal obligation of employers (and others) to take reasonable steps to ensure the health, safety, and welfare of workers. Under the WHS Act 2011.
