Standard Operating Procedure — Australian HR Management
◎ HR Strategy & Workforce Planning
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Develop an HR strategy aligned to business objectives and produce a workforce plan that identifies current capability, future needs, gaps, and actions required to close them.
Scope
All organisations conducting strategic or operational HR planning. At PPG this is led by the CHRO annually with input from all GMs.
Key legislation
Fair Work Act 2009 (s.202-205 consultation); Privacy Act 1988 (workforce data); Workplace Gender Equality Act 2012; Superannuation Guarantee (Administration) Act 1992
Step-by-step process
1
Analyse the business strategy
Obtain and read the current business strategy, strategic plan, and board-approved budget. Identify the 3-5 year growth trajectory, new capabilities required, geographic expansion plans, and technology changes. At PPG: review the six strategic pillars and four HR objectives before any workforce planning begins.
2
Conduct an environmental scan (PESTLE)
Analyse external factors: Political (IR law changes, immigration policy), Economic (wage growth, labour market tightness), Social (workforce demographics), Technological (automation, AI), Legal (Fair Work Act amendments, WGEA obligations), Environmental (ESG commitments). This contextualises the plan and surfaces risks HR needs to plan around.
3
Audit current workforce - supply analysis
Pull HRIS data on current headcount, FTE mix, tenure distribution, age profile, skills inventory, gender breakdown, and turnover rates. Map current capability against the competencies the business needs. At PPG: identify the 74% skills gap in automation systems operation before planning reskilling.
4
Forecast future workforce needs - demand analysis
Work with each department head to forecast headcount requirements over 12, 24, and 36 months. Consider growth targets, productivity improvements, technology changes, and natural attrition. Formula: Future HC = (Current HC x predicted retention rate) + new roles required.
5
Identify gaps by comparing supply and demand
For each department, compare current supply against future demand. Categorise gaps as Quantitative (headcount shortfalls), Qualitative (skills gaps), or Structural (wrong employment mix). At PPG: Operations has a 74% gap in automation skills; Tech has retention-driven supply shortfalls.
6
Develop workforce strategies to close gaps
For each gap, select: Build (train existing staff), Buy (recruit externally), Borrow (contractors or secondments), or Bot (automate the task). Document rationale. Prioritise by urgency and business impact. At PPG: reskilling Operations staff (Build) is preferred over redundancy to manage the AI transition ethically and legally.
7
Build the HR Strategy document
Structure: (1) Executive summary, (2) Business context, (3) Current workforce analysis, (4) Future state vision, (5) Priority HR objectives with 12/24/36 month targets, (6) Initiatives and programs, (7) Resource requirements and budget, (8) KPIs and measurement framework, (9) Risks and mitigations. Align each objective to a business pillar.
8
Consult and validate with stakeholders
Present draft to CEO, CFO, and GM leadership team. Where the strategy involves major workplace change, trigger consultation obligations under the Fair Work Act and EA before finalising. At PPG: CEO Marcus Okafor must not bypass consultation - HR leads this process and ensures legal obligations are met.
9
Obtain approval and communicate
Obtain formal sign-off from CEO and Board if required. Develop a communication plan for sharing the strategy with managers and employees. Summarise key people commitments to build trust. Publish to the intranet or shared drive.
10
Monitor, measure, and review quarterly
Review the strategy quarterly against KPIs: headcount vs plan, turnover vs target, skills gap closure rate, internal promotion rate, engagement score trend. Conduct a formal annual review. Adjust for material changes in the business.
Tools & templates
HRIS workforce data exportPESTLE analysis template9-box talent gridWorkforce supply/demand model (spreadsheet)Gap analysis matrixHR Strategy document templateBalanced scorecard / OKR framework
PPG context — Pinnacle Pacific Group

PPG's FY2025 workforce plan must address three simultaneous pressures: AI/automation transition in Operations (reskilling 198 staff), CFO succession gap (18 months to retirement, no successor), and planned Perth/Adelaide hub (+60 FTE by FY2027). The strategy must link directly to the six business pillars and include WGEA reporting obligations as a non-negotiable constraint.

Practitioner tips
  • ✦A workforce plan without a business strategy to anchor it is just a headcount spreadsheet. Always start with strategy.
  • ✦Use rolling 3-year horizons: year 1 is operational (fill vacancies), year 2 is tactical (build capability), year 3 is strategic (transform workforce).
  • ✦Workforce plans must include total employment cost modelling: base salary + super (11.5% rising to 12% July 2025) + on-costs (~20-25%).
  • ✦Under the Workplace Gender Equality Act 2012, employers with 100+ staff must report workforce composition annually. Your strategy should include targets that close current gaps.
Standard Operating Procedure — Australian HR Management
◈ Recruitment & Selection
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Attract, assess, and appoint candidates on merit using a fair, consistent, and legally compliant process that does not discriminate on protected attributes.
Scope
All permanent, fixed-term, casual, and contractor recruitment across PPG. Applies to all hiring managers and HR staff.
Key legislation
Fair Work Act 2009 (s.536 FWIS; s.340 general protections); Age Discrimination Act 2004; Sex Discrimination Act 1984; Racial Discrimination Act 1975; Disability Discrimination Act 1992; Equal Opportunity Act 2010 (Vic) - positive duty; Privacy Act 1988 (APPs 3, 5, 6, 11); Migration Act 1958
Step-by-step process
1
Obtain written approval to recruit
Before any recruitment activity begins, the hiring manager must obtain written approval from their GM and CHRO confirming: the position is in the workforce plan and budget, the PD is current and HR-reviewed, and the employment type and remuneration band are confirmed. Do not advertise without written approval.
2
Review and update the position description
Confirm the PD accurately reflects the role. HR must review all selection criteria to ensure they are genuinely necessary, non-discriminatory, and compliant with the relevant Award classification. At PPG: physical warehouse criteria must be validated as genuinely necessary to avoid indirect sex discrimination risk.
3
Advertise internally for a minimum of 5 business days
Post the role on the internal jobs board before external advertising. This supports the internal fill rate target (PPG target: 45%, currently 29%). Notify the HRBP to identify internal candidates, high-potential staff, or succession candidates who should be encouraged to apply.
4
Advertise externally if no suitable internal candidate
Advertise using channels appropriate to the role. Ensure all advertisements: state the role accurately, do not include discriminatory language, and comply with the Privacy Act by stating how candidate data will be handled.
5
Shortlist against selection criteria only
Both the hiring manager and HR independently review applications against the selection criteria. Document shortlisting decisions. Do not consider any protected attribute: age, gender, ethnicity, family status, disability, or pregnancy. Any selection decision that cannot be justified against the criteria is a discrimination risk.
6
Conduct structured behavioural interviews (STAR method)
HR develops a structured interview guide using the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result). All candidates must be asked the same core questions. Panels for Level 4+ roles must include at least one HR representative. Do not ask questions that elicit information about protected attributes.
7
Complete pre-employment checks
Complete as appropriate to the role: Working rights verification (all roles - mandatory under Migration Act); Reference checks (minimum two); National Police Check (Transport, Finance, Level 5+); Pre-employment medical (Operations, Transport - must be genuinely necessary). All checks comply with Privacy Act APPs - candidates must be informed upfront.
8
Make selection decision and extend written offer
Hiring manager and HR make the decision jointly, documented against the criteria. Prepare a written offer letter approved by HR including: role title, commencement date, employment type, remuneration (base + super), location, and conditions. Do not make verbal-only offers for permanent roles.
9
Issue all commencement documents on day one
On commencement: issue the Fair Work Information Statement (mandatory under s.536 FWA); have the employment contract signed; provide Casual Employment Information Statement if applicable; complete tax file number declaration and super fund election. Failure to issue the FWIS is a breach of the NES.
10
Manage unsuccessful candidates and records
Notify unsuccessful candidates promptly. Offer brief feedback where appropriate. Destroy or de-identify application materials after 12 months (APP 11). Do not retain candidates in a talent pool without their explicit written consent.
Tools & templates
Position description templateRecruitment approval formCompliant job ad templateStructured interview guide (STAR method)Reference check guideSelection scoring matrixOffer letter templateFair Work Information Statement (fairwork.gov.au)Pre-employment check consent form
PPG context — Pinnacle Pacific Group

PPG internal fill rate is 29% against a 45% target. HR should actively brief Team Leaders and GMs on internal vacancies before external advertising. For Operations roles, physical selection criteria must be validated as genuinely required. PPG conducts police checks and medicals for Transport roles - candidates must be informed upfront under APP 5.

Practitioner tips
  • ✦The Equal Opportunity Act 2010 (Vic) imposes a positive duty - you must proactively design non-discriminatory recruitment, not just react to complaints. Structured interviews and documented decisions are your evidence.
  • ✦Behavioural interview questions (STAR format) are legally stronger than hypothetical questions because they require evidence of past behaviour.
  • ✦You must verify working rights for every person you hire. Knowingly employing someone without work rights is an offence under the Migration Act 1958.
  • ✦A 90-day attrition rate of 11% often signals a recruitment problem: hiring for the wrong fit, overselling the role, or poor onboarding handoff. Audit exit interviews of early leavers.

◉ Onboarding & Induction
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Integrate new employees effectively, ensuring they understand their role, team, culture, obligations, and entitlements from day one - reducing early attrition and accelerating time to productivity.
Scope
All new starters (permanent, fixed-term, casual) at all PPG locations. HR owns the framework; line managers own local delivery.
Key legislation
Fair Work Act 2009 (s.536 - FWIS on commencement); OHS Act 2004 (Vic) / WHS Act 2011 - duty to provide training from day one; Privacy Act 1988; Superannuation Guarantee (Administration) Act 1992
Step-by-step process
1
Complete pre-boarding before day one
Send a welcome email confirming start details. Set up HRIS record, email, system access, and equipment. Send digital paperwork (tax file, super, emergency contacts, bank details). Add to payroll. Brief the line manager and team on the new starter. A warm pre-boarding experience dramatically reduces first-week anxiety.
2
Day 1 - Welcome, administration, and site orientation
Provide a personal welcome with the manager present. Complete remaining paperwork (contract signature, FWIS, Casual FWIS if applicable). Issue ID, access card, keys, and PPE. Conduct a site tour including emergency exits, first aid, and facilities. Introduce to team members individually. Do not leave a new starter at reception unaccompanied.
3
Day 1 - Organisation overview and role context
Cover: PPG history, mission, vision, and values; the organisational structure; the team's purpose; immediate priorities and expectations for the first week; and who to contact for common questions.
4
Days 1-3 - WHS and compliance induction
Mandatory compliance induction must cover: WHS responsibilities under the OHS/WHS Act; hazard reporting procedures; emergency procedures; incident reporting; Safe Work Method Statements for the role; Code of Conduct; and key policies. All completion must be documented and signed. This is a legal obligation, not optional.
5
Week 1 - Role-specific training
Line manager delivers: systems and tools training; key processes and workflows; performance expectations and KPIs; introduction to key stakeholders; any mandatory role-specific licences. At PPG: warehouse roles require documented WMS training and forklift licence verification before unsupervised equipment operation begins.
6
Day 30 - First structured check-in
Manager conducts a structured 30-day check-in. HR Advisor or HRBP sends a separate brief check-in (3-5 questions on role clarity, support, and whether the role matches expectations). Identify any early concerns or support needs. Poor role clarity at day 30 is the leading indicator of 90-day attrition.
7
Day 60 - Mid-probation review
Manager conducts a documented mid-probation review: assess performance against probation objectives; provide specific and constructive feedback. If not on track - initiate a probation support plan immediately. HR must be notified of any probation concerns at 60 days, not at 89 days.
8
Day 90 - Probation confirmation or action
Formal probation review with documented outcome: Confirmed (letter issued, HRIS updated); Extended (written notice, improvement requirements, new review date - HR approval required); Not confirmed (written notice, notice period or payment in lieu per NES, HRBP sign-off required, adverse action risk assessed). General protections still apply during probation.
9
Post-90 - Integration and development
Transition from onboarding to regular performance management. Conduct a 90-day onboarding debrief to feed program improvement. Enrol in ongoing development programs. Add to succession planning inputs if high-potential signals identified.
Tools & templates
Pre-boarding checklistFair Work Information Statement (fairwork.gov.au)Induction completion register30/60/90-day check-in templatesProbation review formBuddy program guideWHS induction sign-off sheetRole-specific training matrix
PPG context — Pinnacle Pacific Group

PPG has a 90-day attrition rate of 11% - nearly one in nine new hires leaves within 3 months. Jordan Bell (Head of People Experience) has identified broken onboarding as the root cause. Key fixes required: manager briefing before day 1, buddy assignment at offer stage, structured 30/60-day check-ins, and a role-specific training matrix for Operations.

Practitioner tips
  • ✦Onboarding starts at offer acceptance, not day one. Pre-boarding engagement dramatically reduces first-week anxiety.
  • ✦The leading indicator of 90-day attrition is role clarity at day 30. If someone cannot clearly describe what they do and why it matters, they are a flight risk.
  • ✦Buddy programs reduce early attrition by 20-50%. The buddy should be a peer (not the manager), trained, and willing.
  • ✦Failure to issue the Fair Work Information Statement on commencement is a breach of the NES under s.536.
Standard Operating Procedure — Australian HR Management
◐ Performance Management
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Manage and develop employee performance fairly, consistently, and in compliance with the Fair Work Act - focused on early intervention, genuine support, and meticulous documentation.
Scope
All employees at PPG. Casual and fixed-term employees during their engagement.
Key legislation
Fair Work Act 2009 (s.387 unfair dismissal two-part test; Part 3-1 general protections; reverse onus); Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (reasonable adjustment); Fair Work Regulations 2009 (reg.1.07 serious misconduct); OHS Act 2004 / WHS Act 2011 (psychosocial hazards)
Step-by-step process
1
Set clear expectations at commencement
Performance management starts at onboarding, not when things go wrong. Every employee must have: a current PD with clear KPIs; a documented probation plan for new starters; a Personal Development Plan reviewed at least twice yearly; and clarity on what meeting expectations looks like. Without this baseline, formal performance action is legally vulnerable.
2
Conduct regular documented 1:1 check-ins
Managers must conduct monthly 1:1 meetings (documented using the 1:1 template). These are two-way conversations: employee-led agenda items plus manager feedback. Use check-ins to identify performance concerns early. Document observations, agreed actions, and any concerns. This record is critical evidence if formal action is later required.
3
Raise concerns informally at Stage 1
As soon as a performance concern is identified, the manager raises it directly in a private conversation. Be specific: describe the behaviour or gap, the expected standard, and the impact. Give the employee a genuine opportunity to respond. Document the date, what was discussed, and agreed next steps. Do not skip this stage - it is the first record in the performance file.
4
Check for reasonable adjustment obligations
Before any formal step, assess: Has the employee disclosed a disability, injury, or medical condition? Could a health or personal issue be contributing? If yes, explore reasonable adjustments first (modified duties, flexible hours, adjusted targets, EAP referral). Failure to consider reasonable adjustment before formal performance action risks a Disability Discrimination Act claim even if the performance concern is genuine.
5
Issue a first written warning at Stage 2
If informal coaching has not produced improvement (typically 2-4 weeks): (a) Notify the employee in advance of the formal meeting; (b) Conduct the meeting - the employee has the right to a support person; (c) Issue a written warning stating the specific concern, expected standard, support provided, improvement timeframe, and consequences; (d) Document the employee's response and your genuine consideration of it; (e) Obtain HRBP sign-off before issuing.
6
Implement a Performance Improvement Plan at Stage 3
Develop a PIP with: specific, measurable objectives (not vague aspirations); a clear timeframe (30-90 days); weekly documented check-ins; concrete support mechanisms; and explicit measurement criteria. HRBP must be involved in all PIPs. CHRO must approve. Issue in writing and obtain the employee's signature (receipt only, not agreement).
7
Conduct weekly PIP check-ins and document progress
Every weekly PIP check-in must be documented: date, attendees, progress rating against each objective, employee's comments, any adjustments. This record is the most important evidence in any subsequent Fair Work Commission proceeding. If the employee is improving, document that in writing too.
8
Determine the PIP outcome and proceed accordingly
At completion: Objectives met - confirm in writing, return to normal cycle; Partial improvement - consider a documented extension; Objectives not met - proceed to final warning or termination (CHRO approval required). Before terminating: confirm valid reason; confirm procedural fairness steps are documented; assess general protections risks.
9
Issue a show cause letter before termination
Issue a show cause letter stating the performance history; invite the employee to show cause in writing; allow reasonable time to respond (24-48 hours); genuinely consider the response before deciding. Issuing a termination letter before reading the show cause response is a procedural fairness failure.
10
Issue the termination letter and finalise
Prepare a written termination letter (valid reason, effective date, notice period or payment in lieu per NES/Award minimum). Conduct a private meeting with a support person available. Arrange property return, finalise entitlements, process HRIS termination same day, conduct exit interview (HRBP-led, not the terminating manager).
Tools & templates
1:1 meeting templatePDP templateFirst written warning templatePerformance Improvement Plan templateShow cause letter templateTermination letter templatePerformance file checklistEAP referral form
PPG context — Pinnacle Pacific Group

Aaliyah Wong is currently managing 4 active performance/ER cases in Operations. Key risks: Sandra Cortez wants to skip stages under operational pressure - HRBP must hold the line. Workers raising WHS complaints before or during a PIP creates adverse action risk - document that the PIP was based on pre-existing data. Operations TLs are managing first-level conduct matters without sufficient HR support.

Practitioner tips
  • ✦The s.387 two-part test: (1) Was there a valid reason? AND (2) Was procedural fairness followed? You can win on reason and lose on procedure - and still pay compensation.
  • ✦The reverse onus in general protections means if an employee claims adverse action, you must prove the action was not for a prohibited reason. Document your reasons before you take each step.
  • ✦A PIP should never be a surprise. If a PIP comes as a shock to the employee, you have skipped informal coaching stages and the process is legally fragile.
  • ✦Never issue a formal warning or PIP on the same day an employee lodges a grievance or makes a WHS complaint. Document the independence of the performance concern.

◑ Learning & Development
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Systematically identify, design, deliver, and evaluate learning programs that build individual capability, close workforce skills gaps, and support PPG strategic objectives.
Scope
All employees at PPG. Covers mandatory compliance training, role-specific technical training, leadership development, and career development.
Key legislation
OHS Act 2004 (Vic) / WHS Act 2011 - duty to provide training and instruction; National VET Regulator Act 2011 (ASQA/AQF); Fair Work Act 2009 (EA provisions for study leave); Privacy Act 1988 (training records as personal information)
Step-by-step process
1
Conduct a Training Needs Analysis (TNA)
A TNA identifies the gap between current and required capability. Three inputs: (1) Organisational level - review the workforce plan for strategic capability gaps (e.g. automation systems, change leadership); (2) Role level - compare PDs and competency frameworks against current performance; (3) Individual level - use PDP reviews and performance data. At PPG: the skills audit shows a 74% gap in automation systems operation - this drives the highest-priority L&D investment.
2
Prioritise training by type and urgency
Categorise training: Mandatory/compliance (legally required - WHS induction, high-risk work licences); Mandatory/operational (required to perform the role - WMS, forklift); Development (skills improvement); Strategic (capability for future roles). Prioritise mandatory training as non-negotiable. Development and strategic training are planned in the L&D calendar and PDP.
3
Map training to AQF qualifications where possible
Where training aligns to a nationally recognised qualification, engage an ASQA-registered RTO. Only RTOs can issue qualifications and statements of attainment under the AQF. At PPG: warehouse automation reskilling should target Certificate III in Warehousing Operations or Certificate IV in Leadership and Management.
4
Design or procure the training solution
Decide build vs buy: Build (internal facilitation) for role-specific content; Buy (RTO or e-learning) for nationally recognised content or compliance training. For in-house design: use adult learning principles (relevant, practical, learner-driven, immediately applicable); mix formats (face-to-face, e-learning, on-the-job coaching, mentoring).
5
Schedule and communicate the L&D calendar
Build a 12-month calendar aligned to the HR strategy. Schedule mandatory training first. For Operations shift workers: schedule across shifts to avoid under-resourcing. If mandatory training is scheduled outside ordinary hours, check Award/EA - overtime or penalty rates may apply.
6
Deliver training and document all completion
Ensure the facilitator is suitably qualified. Use sign-in sheets or LMS records. After delivery: update the training register; upload certificates to HRIS; notify manager of completion; follow up non-completers for mandatory training. Training records are legal documents in a WHS investigation - date, attendees, content, facilitator, and outcome must all be documented.
7
Evaluate training effectiveness using Kirkpatrick's four levels
(1) Reaction - did participants find it relevant? (post-survey same day); (2) Learning - did they gain the skill? (pre/post assessments); (3) Behaviour - are they applying it on the job? (manager observation, 30-day follow-up); (4) Results - has it improved business outcomes? (KPI data). Report L&D ROI to the CHRO quarterly.
8
Manage mandatory licence and certification expiries
Maintain a licences register for all roles requiring mandatory credentials (forklift LF, EWP, HR/MC, first aid). Set automated reminders at 60 and 30 days before expiry. A worker operating equipment without a current valid licence is a WHS breach regardless of their actual competency.
9
Review and update the L&D program annually
Annual review: compare training hours/FTE against target; review L&D spend/FTE ROI; update the TNA based on new business priorities; review RTO provider performance; update compliance training for legislative changes. Present to the CHRO for approval.
Tools & templates
Training Needs Analysis templateL&D calendar templateTraining register / LMSRTO procurement checklistKirkpatrick evaluation templateLicences and certifications registerPDP template (links to L&D goals)AQF qualification level guide
PPG context — Pinnacle Pacific Group

PPG spends $1,240/FTE on L&D (18.2 hrs/FTE). As warehouse automation rolls out, HR must build a training matrix for new systems - training on automation equipment is a legal WHS obligation. Reskilling should be tied to AQF credentials to reduce employee resistance to change. EA 2027 negotiations are an opportunity to embed training entitlements as a bargaining offering.

Practitioner tips
  • ✦Training records are legal documents in a WHS investigation. 'We trained them' is only a defence if you can prove it with date, attendees, content, facilitator, and outcome.
  • ✦High-risk work licences are state-regulated. Ensure licences are current and issued by an approved assessor - not just an internal tick-off.
  • ✦The most effective L&D combines formal training with structured on-the-job application and manager coaching. Training alone achieves less than 10% behaviour change (Ebbinghaus forgetting curve).
  • ✦If investing in leadership development for the High Potential Accelerator, link it explicitly to succession outcomes - a visible career path is more motivating than a generic leadership course.
Standard Operating Procedure — Australian HR Management
◆ Employee Relations & ER Case Management
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Manage workplace complaints, disputes, and ER matters fairly, impartially, and in compliance with applicable laws - resolving issues at the lowest appropriate level while protecting all parties.
Scope
All employees at all PPG locations. HRBP owns ER case management for Operations and Transport; the CHRO manages executive-level matters.
Key legislation
Fair Work Act 2009 (Part 3-1 general protections; s.789FD bullying; Part 3-2 unfair dismissal; s.340 adverse action); Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Respect@Work 2022 - positive duty); Equal Opportunity Act 2010 (Vic); Racial Discrimination Act 1975; Disability Discrimination Act 1992; OHS Act 2004 / WHS Act 2011
Step-by-step process
1
Receive and triage the complaint or matter
Acknowledge within 2 business days and categorise: (a) Interpersonal conflict - informal resolution first; (b) Bullying/harassment - formal investigation likely required; (c) Sexual harassment - formal investigation required under Respect@Work positive duty; (d) Discrimination - formal investigation; (e) Misconduct/performance - performance management pathway; (f) Grievance about a management decision - grievance process; (g) Suspected underpayment - immediate payroll investigation. The triage determines the pathway.
2
Take immediate interim action where required
If the situation poses ongoing risk: separate the parties by rostering or temporary redeployment; refer the affected employee to EAP; brief managers on confidentiality obligations; consider whether the respondent needs to be stood down on full pay pending investigation (only for serious misconduct). Document all interim actions and the reasons for them.
3
Determine whether a formal investigation is required
Consider: Is this a one-off interpersonal disagreement (mediation may suffice)? Is there an allegation of a breach of policy or law (formal investigation required)? For formal investigations: use an external investigator for senior staff, complex matters, or where internal HR has a conflict of interest.
4
Plan and scope the investigation
Document before commencing: the specific allegations; the evidence to be gathered; the witnesses to be interviewed (complainant first, then witnesses, then respondent); the investigator's identity and authority; and the expected timeframe (aim for completion within 20 business days). Brief the relevant GM on category and timeframe only - not the substance.
5
Conduct interviews following the rules of natural justice
Interview sequence: complainant first, then witnesses, then respondent. For each interview: allow a support person to be present; take detailed contemporaneous notes; provide a summary for the interviewee to review and confirm. Do not share one party's account verbatim with another. The respondent must know the full substance of the allegations and have a genuine opportunity to respond - this is natural justice.
6
Gather and assess all relevant evidence
Gather documentary evidence: timesheets, rosters, emails, CCTV footage, prior complaint records, performance history. Assess credibility of each piece of evidence. Apply the civil standard of proof: balance of probabilities (more likely than not that the conduct occurred). Document your reasoning for accepting or rejecting each piece of evidence.
7
Make findings and determine outcome
Make a finding for each allegation: Substantiated, Unsubstantiated, or Inconclusive. Do not make findings beyond the scope of the investigation. Prepare a confidential written investigation report. Outcomes: Substantiated - disciplinary action proportionate to severity; Unsubstantiated - no adverse action against complainant; Inconclusive - consider structural changes to reduce recurrence.
8
Communicate outcomes to both parties
Tell the complainant: investigation is complete, whether the complaint was substantiated, and what action is being taken in general terms. Tell the respondent: findings and action being taken. Both communications in person with a support person available, followed by written confirmation. Do not share the investigation report itself with either party.
9
Implement disciplinary action if required
Disciplinary action must follow the Performance Management SOP procedural steps: show cause, genuine consideration, decision, written outcome. Ensure action is proportionate to the finding. For serious misconduct: summary dismissal is possible after investigation confirms the finding - but procedural fairness still applies. Obtain CHRO approval for any termination.
10
Follow up and monitor for recurrence
Check in with the complainant 4 weeks after resolution to confirm no recurrence or retaliation. Monitor the working environment for ongoing tension. Update the complaints register. Review whether systemic causes should be addressed. Close the case in HRIS and retain all records securely for 7 years.

PPG context — Pinnacle Pacific Group

Aaliyah Wong has 4 active ER/performance cases in Operations. Key risks: Sandra Cortez must not be involved in investigations of her direct reports (conflict of interest); any performance action against someone who recently raised a complaint needs documented pre-existing performance evidence; the low psychological safety score (63/100) suggests likely unreported complaints. The Respect@Work positive duty means HR must proactively assess harassment risk - not wait for complaints.

Practitioner tips
  • ✦The most common ER mistake in Australia: conflating interpersonal conflict with bullying. Bullying requires repeated, unreasonable behaviour. A single incident is not bullying - but may be harassment. The pathway is different.
  • ✦Natural justice has two limbs: the right to know the case against you, and the right to respond. Both must be genuinely met. If the outcome was predetermined, it is not procedurally fair.
  • ✦Under Respect@Work (2022), the AHRC can investigate employers proactively without a complaint. A low psychological safety score in a male-dominated environment warrants a proactive risk assessment.
  • ✦Retain all ER case records for a minimum of 7 years. General protections claims can be lodged at any time, and historical conduct patterns can surface in proceedings.
Standard Operating Procedure — Australian HR Management
◕ WHS & Psychosocial Hazard Management
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Identify, assess, and control both physical and psychosocial workplace hazards, manage incidents and injuries, and support injured workers back to work - ensuring PPG meets its legal obligations across all three jurisdictions.
Scope
All workers (employees, contractors, labour hire, visitors) at all PPG sites. Multi-jurisdictional: Melbourne (OHS Act Vic), Sydney (WHS Act NSW), Brisbane (WHS Act Qld).
Key legislation
OHS Act 2004 (Vic) - employer primary duty, WorkSafe Victoria; WHS Act 2011 (Cth model - NSW, Qld) - PCBU duty; WHS Regulations 2023 - psychosocial hazards; WIRC Act 2013 (Vic); Workers Compensation Act 1987 (NSW); WCRA 2003 (Qld); Safe Work Australia Model Code: Managing Psychosocial Hazards at Work
Step-by-step process
1
Establish WHS governance and responsibilities
Document and communicate WHS responsibilities: PCBU/Employer - ensure health and safety so far as reasonably practicable; Officers (CEO, GMs) - exercise due diligence (acquire WHS knowledge, verify controls, ensure resources); Managers/supervisors - implement controls, report incidents; Workers - take reasonable care, follow procedures, report hazards; HR - own psychosocial hazard management and RTW for psychological injuries.
2
Identify physical hazards systematically
Physical hazard identification methods: workplace inspections (monthly for Operations, quarterly for corporate); pre-start and toolbox talks; incident and near-miss data analysis; equipment and plant risk assessments; Safe Work Method Statements (SWMS) for high-risk activities; consultation with elected Health and Safety Representatives (HSRs). Record all hazards in the hazard register. Prioritise by risk rating: likelihood x consequence.
3
Identify psychosocial hazards - a 2023 legal obligation
Psychosocial hazards must now be formally identified, assessed, and controlled under the 2023 WHS Regulations. Methods: validated employee surveys (People at Work survey - free from safeworkaustralia.gov.au); focus groups; analysis of engagement data, absenteeism, and incidents; exit interview themes; grievance patterns. At PPG: formally assessed hazards include high job demands (8.4% overtime), change anxiety (AI rollout), low job control, bullying/harassment risk (63/100 psychological safety), and isolated work (Transport drivers).
4
Assess and prioritise risks using the hierarchy of controls
For each hazard, apply the hierarchy in order: (1) Elimination - remove the hazard entirely; (2) Substitution - replace with a less hazardous option; (3) Isolation - separate the hazard from workers; (4) Engineering controls - physical barriers, guarding; (5) Administrative controls - procedures, training, rosters; (6) PPE - last resort. For psychosocial hazards: organisational-level controls (workload management, role clarity, consultation) are far more effective than individual supports (EAP access alone is not a sufficient control).
5
Implement controls and verify effectiveness
Document controls in the risk register with: control type, implementation date, owner, and verification method. Verify high-risk controls are in place before work commences. For psychosocial controls: check effectiveness at 3 months using engagement and absenteeism data. If controls are not reducing risk, escalate to the next level of the hierarchy.
6
Manage notifiable incidents and regulatory reporting
Notifiable incidents must be reported IMMEDIATELY by phone: death of a worker; serious injury or illness; dangerous incident. Steps: (1) Make scene safe; (2) Call emergency services if required; (3) Notify regulator by phone immediately - WorkSafe Vic: 13 23 60 / SafeWork NSW: 13 10 50 / WHS Qld: 1300 362 128; (4) Do not disturb the scene without regulator approval; (5) Submit written notification within 48 hours. Failure to report immediately is a criminal offence.
7
Conduct incident investigation and root cause analysis
For all incidents and near-misses: (1) Gather evidence immediately before scene is disturbed; (2) Conduct root cause analysis - not just what happened but why the system allowed it; (3) Identify contributing factors (human, environmental, system/procedural); (4) Use the 5 Whys or fishbone method; (5) Develop corrective actions targeting root causes; (6) Assign owners with due dates; (7) Verify completion and effectiveness. Complete within 5 business days for non-notifiable incidents.
8
Manage workers compensation claims and return to work
When a claim is lodged: (1) Accept the claim - do not manage informally; (2) Notify your workers comp insurer within the required timeframe (Vic: within 5 days); (3) Appoint a RTW coordinator for serious injuries; (4) Obtain a Certificate of Capacity from the treating GP; (5) Develop a RTW plan with suitable duties in consultation with the worker, doctor, and insurer. Legal obligation: you must provide suitable employment for an injured worker returning to work. Terminating a worker on workers compensation is extremely high-risk.
9
Manage psychological injury claims with additional care
Psychological injury claims require: (1) Accept the claim without requiring proof that the injury was work-caused; (2) Follow all treating psychologist RTW restrictions including restrictions around specific individuals; (3) Early intervention (EAP, modified duties, flexible hours) reduces claim duration; (4) Do not continue the stressor during RTW - same manager, same workload. At PPG: if a psychological injury is linked to a specific manager, that manager must not be involved in the RTW process.
10
Consult workers and conduct an annual WHS program review
Legal obligation: both the OHS Act (Vic) and WHS Act require consultation with workers on health and safety matters through elected HSRs (who have legal powers including Provisional Improvement Notices and work cessation directions), WHS committees, and toolbox talks. Annual WHS review: analyse incident rates, near-miss trends, workers comp costs, and psychosocial survey data. Report to executive quarterly.

◈ Change Management & Consultation
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Plan and implement organisational change in a way that meets legal consultation obligations, manages employee wellbeing, and achieves sustainable adoption - particularly where technology, structure, or workforce composition is changing.
Scope
Any significant change at PPG: technology changes (AI/automation), structural changes (new hub, restructure), role changes, EA variations, leadership changes, or workforce reductions.
Key legislation
Fair Work Act 2009 (s.202-205 major workplace change consultation; EA consultation term; s.228 good faith bargaining); OHS Act 2004 (Vic) / WHS Act 2011 (worker consultation on safety changes); WHS Regulations 2023 (change anxiety is a regulated psychosocial hazard); Privacy Act 1988; NES redundancy provisions
Step-by-step process
1
Classify the change and determine consultation obligations
Determine: (1) Major workplace change - 'a change likely to have a significant effect on employees' including technology changes affecting roles and restructures. This triggers mandatory consultation under the EA consultation term and s.202 FWA. (2) Operational change - routine change without significant effect - consultation is good practice but not legally required. At PPG: the AI/automation rollout and Perth/Adelaide expansion are both Major Workplace Changes. Consultation is mandatory, not optional.
2
Build a change management plan before communicating anything
Before communicating to employees, plan: (1) Scope - what exactly is changing, for whom, and when?; (2) Drivers - why is the change necessary? Be honest; (3) Impact assessment - which roles are affected?; (4) Consultation timeline - when will you engage employees with genuine time to influence the outcome?; (5) Communication plan; (6) Support measures (EAP, reskilling, redeployment options); (7) Risks and mitigations. Consultation must be planned before the decision is finalised.
3
Issue written notice to affected employees
As soon as the change is proposed (before it is final), provide written notice to affected employees including: the nature of the change; expected effects on employees; timing; how they can provide input; and who to contact. If employees are union members, the union must also be formally notified. At PPG: Operations staff are 61% union members - the union delegate must be formally invited to participate in consultation.
4
Conduct genuine consultation - not notification
Consultation means informing employees early enough that their input can actually change the outcome. Required steps: (1) Hold meetings with affected employees in small groups; (2) Provide meaningful information; (3) Invite employees and representatives to discuss and propose alternatives; (4) Genuinely consider every alternative raised and document your consideration; (5) Keep consultation open for a reasonable period. Telling employees after the decision is made is notification, not consultation. This is a breach of the Fair Work Act.
5
Address psychosocial hazards from change anxiety
Change anxiety is a formally regulated psychosocial hazard under 2023 WHS Regulations. Actions: identify who is most at risk; provide clear, honest, regular communication (uncertainty is more stressful than bad news); promote EAP access; brief managers on supportive conversations; monitor absenteeism and engagement indicators; conduct a psychosocial hazard assessment and implement specific controls. At PPG: Operations absenteeism spiked during the AI announcement - this is a WHS signal requiring formal action.
6
Manage redeployment before declaring any redundancy
Where roles are being eliminated: (1) Before declaring a redundancy, conduct a genuine search for suitable alternative positions; (2) Consult the employee about alternative roles; (3) Consider retraining for the alternative role; (4) Only if no suitable redeployment exists and consultation obligations are met can the redundancy be declared genuine. A redundancy is not genuine if the employer could have redeployed the employee but chose not to. At PPG: reskilling warehouse staff for automation operator roles is a redeployment option that must be documented as genuinely explored.
7
Communicate the final decision clearly and simultaneously
After consultation is complete: communicate the final decision to all affected employees simultaneously; acknowledge what feedback was received and how it influenced the outcome; be specific about what happens next and when; communicate the support available. Also communicate to non-affected employees - change anxiety is contagious in a team environment.
8
Support employees through the transition
During implementation: maintain regular communication updates (weekly during active change); monitor absenteeism, engagement, and performance data; provide the reskilling and transition support committed during consultation; check in with managers (they are often the most stressed and least supported in a change program); conduct a post-change review at 90 days.

Practitioner tips
  • ✦The single most common change management legal error in Australia: telling employees about a change after the decision is made and calling it consultation. Consultation must occur while the employer can still be influenced.
  • ✦The ADKAR change model (Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement) is a practical Australian-used framework. Every employee needs all five stages for change to stick. Most programs stop at Awareness.
  • ✦Resistance to change is usually about fear of the unknown or distrust of management, not the change itself. Transparent, honest, early communication is the highest-ROI change management investment.
  • ✦If a union is involved, good faith bargaining obligations apply. Surface bargaining (going through the motions without genuine intent) is a breach of s.228 FWA.

◔ Termination, Redundancy & Separation
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Manage all forms of employment separation - resignation, performance dismissal, serious misconduct, and redundancy - in a legally compliant, fair, and dignified manner.
Scope
All forms of separation at PPG: voluntary resignation, dismissal (performance, conduct, serious misconduct), genuine redundancy, retirement, fixed-term contract end, and death in service.
Key legislation
Fair Work Act 2009 (Part 3-2 unfair dismissal s.387; Part 3-1 general protections s.340; NES Part 2-2 redundancy pay and notice; s.117 notice of termination); NES minimum notice table (s.117); NES redundancy pay scale (s.119); Fair Work Regulations 2009 (reg.1.07 serious misconduct); WIRC Act 2013 (Vic); Long Service Leave Act 2018 (Vic); Privacy Act 1988
Step-by-step process
1
Classify the type of separation
Process varies significantly by type: (1) Voluntary resignation - employee initiated, minimum notice applies; (2) Performance/conduct dismissal - completed performance management process required; (3) Serious misconduct - summary dismissal possible after investigation, procedural fairness still applies; (4) Genuine redundancy - role no longer required, consultation met, redeployment considered; (5) End of fixed-term - contract expiry, no redundancy pay unless a series of contracts suggests ongoing employment; (6) Mutual separation by agreement - usually involves a deed of release. Classify correctly before beginning.
2
Run the pre-dismissal checklist for all dismissals
Before any dismissal confirm: Valid reason documented and defensible?; Employee notified of the reason in writing?; Genuine show cause opportunity provided?; Employee's response genuinely considered?; Support person offered at all formal meetings?; HRBP and CHRO sign-off obtained?; General protections risk assessed (has the employee recently exercised a workplace right)?; Minimum employment period met (6 months for unfair dismissal eligibility)?; High income threshold checked (~$175K per annum)?
3
Confirm the genuine redundancy test is met
A genuine redundancy requires three elements: (1) The employer no longer requires the job to be done by anyone - the role is redundant, not just the person; (2) Consultation obligations have been complied with; (3) It was not reasonable in all circumstances to redeploy the employee to another position within the enterprise. If any element is missing, the redundancy is not genuine and the employee can claim unfair dismissal. Document evidence of all three elements for each affected employee.
4
Issue the termination or redundancy letter
The separation letter must include: employee name and role; date of separation; the reason for separation (mandatory for all dismissals); notice period or payment in lieu (per NES minimum table or Award/EA - the higher entitlement applies); entitlements to be paid; return of company property instructions; reference to post-employment obligations; and date final pay will be processed. Deliver the letter in person, in a private setting, with a support person available. Never dismiss by email, text, or voicemail.
5
Calculate and process all final entitlements
Final pay must include: base salary to date of termination; annual leave balance at the termination rate (including leave loading if applicable under Award); long service leave (Vic: employees with 7+ years entitled to pro-rata LSL on resignation or dismissal); payment in lieu of notice if not working out the period (taxed as ordinary income); redundancy pay (NES scale - taxed as ETP at concessional rate); super on all final payments. Issue a final payslip and ETP payment summary.
6
Manage the notice period or immediate stand-down
If serving notice: maintain normal conduct expectations; ensure knowledge transfer is completed; maintain appropriate system access for handover; brief the team on the departure at an agreed time. If paying in lieu of notice: terminate all system and site access on the last working day; notify IT, payroll, and building security immediately.
7
Conduct a structured exit interview
Conduct with HRBP, not the line manager. Structured questions covering: why the employee is leaving; what PPG does well; what it could improve; whether they experienced harassment or discrimination (important for Respect@Work positive duty); whether their manager was effective. Record responses anonymously in exit data. Analyse quarterly to identify patterns. At PPG: exit data from Operations should be driving retention interventions, not just being filed.
8
Complete all post-separation administration
After the employee leaves: (1) Process termination record in HRIS on the day; (2) Confirm final pay is correct; (3) Disable all system access (email, HRIS, WMS, building access); (4) Ensure final super payment is made in the correct cycle; (5) Agree reference process with the employee; (6) Retain the employee file for 7 years (Fair Work record-keeping requirements), then destroy securely per the Privacy Act.
9
Manage the risk of post-separation claims
Common post-separation claims: Unfair dismissal (FWC - must be lodged within 21 days of dismissal) - mitigated by documented procedural fairness; General protections (FWC/court - no time limit for court) - mitigated by documented reasons independent of any workplace right; Underpayment (FWO - 6-year limitation) - mitigated by accurate payroll and self-audits; Discrimination (AHRC/VEOHRC) - mitigated by merit-based documented decision-making. If a claim is received: notify your employment practices liability insurer; do not contact the former employee directly; engage an employment lawyer before responding.

◉ HR Reporting & Metrics Analysis
PPG FY2025 Australian Standards
Purpose
Establish a regular HR reporting cadence that gives business leaders accurate, timely, and actionable people data - enabling evidence-based decisions on workforce investment, risk management, and strategic planning.
Scope
All HR metrics data at PPG. HR Advisor owns data quality in the HRIS; HRBP owns operational reporting; CHRO owns strategic reporting to the CEO and Board.
Key legislation
Workplace Gender Equality Act 2012 (mandatory WGEA reporting - 31 May deadline); Fair Work Act 2009 (record-keeping obligations s.535-536 - retain employment records for 7 years); Privacy Act 1988 (APPs 3, 6, 10, 11 - workforce data as personal information); Superannuation Guarantee (Administration) Act 1992 (SGC reporting)
Step-by-step process
1
Define your HR metrics framework
Organise metrics into four categories: (1) Compliance metrics - legally required (WGEA gender pay gap, Award compliance, super payment dates); (2) Operational metrics - HR function efficiency (time to fill, cost per hire, onboarding completion rate); (3) Workforce metrics - workforce health (turnover, absence, engagement, internal promotion rate); (4) Strategic metrics - HR linked to business outcomes (revenue per employee, labour cost as % of revenue, skills gap closure rate).
2
Ensure HRIS data quality before reporting
Data quality standards: every active employee record must have current role, department, location, employment type, start date, remuneration, and manager; termination records must be processed same day (late processing distorts headcount and turnover data); leave records updated from payroll in real time; training records uploaded within 5 business days of completion. Conduct a quarterly HRIS audit: pull a report of all records with missing mandatory fields and assign the HR Advisor to clean data before end of quarter.
3
Build and maintain a monthly HR dashboard
The monthly dashboard must include: Headcount (total FTE by department and location vs budget); Vacancies (open positions by department and days open); Turnover (monthly and rolling 12-month rate, voluntary vs involuntary); Time to fill (average days by role type); Absenteeism (unplanned absence rate by department); ER cases (active by type and stage); Training compliance (% mandatory training completed); Engagement (pulse score if running monthly). Keep it visual, concise, and consistent month-to-month so leaders can track trends.
4
Calculate HR metrics using consistent formulas
Key formulas: Turnover rate = (Separations / Average headcount) x 100; Retention rate = 100 - turnover rate; Absence rate = (Days absent / (Headcount x Working days)) x 100; Cost per hire = Total recruitment costs / Number of hires; Time to fill = Date offer accepted - Date vacancy approved; Internal fill rate = Internal appointments / Total appointments x 100; Revenue per employee = Total revenue / Average FTE; Promotion rate = Number promoted / Average headcount x 100. Always define the period consistently - inconsistent period definitions make trend analysis meaningless.
5
Conduct quarterly deep-dive analysis
Monthly dashboards show the 'what'. Quarterly deep-dives explore the 'why': Turnover deep-dive - analyse exit interview data by department, level, tenure group, and manager; identify which factors most predict departure. Engagement deep-dive - cross-tabulate scores by department, level, gender, and tenure; identify which drivers are most correlated with turnover. Skills gap progress - compare skills audit results against the previous quarter; identify which L&D interventions are closing gaps.
6
Produce the annual WGEA report by 31 May
Steps: (1) Pull HRIS data by 1 April to allow time for analysis; (2) Calculate gender pay gaps at each level; (3) Review with CHRO and legal if gaps are material; (4) Submit via WGEA portal (wgea.gov.au) by 31 May; (5) Prepare for public disclosure - WGEA publishes employer-level data. Required data across 6 Gender Equality Indicators: workforce composition; management composition; equal remuneration; flexible work availability; consultation on gender equality; sexual harassment policies.
7
Comply with Fair Work record-keeping obligations
Under s.535-536 FWA, keep employment records for 7 years: employee details (name, commencement date); remuneration (rate, gross and net amounts, deductions); hours worked (for Award-covered employees); leave (accrual, taken, balance); super contributions; termination records. Records must be accessible to a Fair Work Inspector on request; in English; and not altered. Failure to keep records is a civil penalty. If records are inadequate, the onus shifts to the employer to disprove any underpayment allegation.
8
Report to the CEO and Board on people metrics
CEO reporting (monthly): headline dashboard metrics vs targets; key ER risks and active cases summary; turnover trend and cost impact; recruitment pipeline for critical vacancies; L&D compliance rate. Board reporting (quarterly): workforce composition and diversity metrics; WGEA progress; engagement trend; succession coverage for critical roles; significant ER matters in general terms; HR strategy KPI progress. The Board report should be forward-looking as well as retrospective.
9
Use data to drive action, not just report it
For each metric that is off-target: identify the root cause (not just the symptom); propose a specific, time-bound intervention; assign ownership; define the success measure; track at the next reporting cycle. At PPG: Operations turnover (22%) has been tracked but not actioned. Data should be triggering a targeted retention intervention - not just appearing in the monthly dashboard as a red metric.

Practitioner tips
  • ✦Under the Fair Work Act, the burden of proof in an underpayment dispute is on the employer to show they paid correctly. If your records are incomplete, you effectively lose by default. Record-keeping is your legal defence.
  • ✦HR metrics without benchmarks are just numbers. Always present in context: PPG turnover 22% vs logistics industry average 14%, national average 12%. Context drives urgency and investment decisions.
  • ✦Workforce data is personal information under the Privacy Act. You can use it for workforce planning (APP 6 - reasonable secondary use) but cannot share individual salary data externally without consent.
  • ✦The most powerful HR metric is not the one that impresses the Board - it is the one that changes what managers do on Monday morning. Design reports for action, not admiration.
